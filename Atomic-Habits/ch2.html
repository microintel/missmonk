You don't rise to your goals.
You fall to who you are.

This page explains, why good habits are hard to keep and bad habits are hard to break. The short answer: most people try to change the wrong thing. We'll walk through it using three friends — Vignesh, Pranav, and Dhanush.

02 — Two Ways to Change

Same goal, two different starting points

You can chase a result, or you can become a person who naturally gets that result. Watch the difference between three friends who all want to get healthier.

Outcome-based

Vignesh

Vignesh wants six-pack abs. He downloads a diet plan and forces himself to follow it. His motivation comes from the goal, so when the goal feels far away, his motivation runs out too.

"I'm trying to eat healthy this month."
Identity-based

Pranav

Pranav doesn't focus on a number on the scale. He decided, "I am a healthy person." Now every choice — walking instead of taking a cab, choosing a salad — is just him acting like himself.

"I'm not someone who skips workouts."
Identity in motion

Dhanush

Dhanush used to bite his nails for years. He booked a manicure — not to save money, but for a small win. His nails looked good, he felt proud, and that pride made him protect the new identity: "I take care of my hands now."

"I've never chewed my nails since."
03 — How to Actually Change

A simple two-step process

You don't need a big dramatic transformation. You need small proof, repeated often.

1

Decide who you want to be

Not "I want to run a marathon" but "I want to be a runner." Not "I want to write a book" but "I want to be a writer." The identity comes first, the goal follows.

2

Prove it with small wins

Every time you do the habit, you are casting a vote for that identity. One vote doesn't decide the election. But votes add up — and you don't need every single one, just most of them.

04 — Chapter Summary

What to do, and what not to do

Written in plain English so anyone can use this right away.

Do this

  • Decide who you want to become before you set a goal. Example: "I want to be a reader," not just "I want to read one book."
  • Do small habits every day. Each one is a small "vote" for the person you're becoming.
  • Say "I am not a smoker" instead of "I am trying to quit." Speak like the new identity already exists.
  • Celebrate small wins, even tiny ones like Dhanush's manicure. Pride helps habits stick.
  • Allow your beliefs about yourself to change over time. Growth means updating your self-image.
  • Judge your progress by your habits, not just by results. Aim to win the majority of your votes, not all of them.

Don't do this

  • Don't only chase outcomes ("lose 10kg") without changing daily actions and beliefs.
  • Don't say "I'm just bad at this" and stop trying. That old story can block new habits.
  • Don't expect one big dramatic change. Real change is small and repeated, not instant.
  • Don't give up after missing one day. One missed vote does not lose the election.
  • Don't hold on tightly to an old identity that no longer helps you (like "I'm always late").
  • Don't copy someone else's habit blindly — connect it to who you actually want to become.
"The goal is not to run a marathon. The goal is to become a runner." — Every habit is a small vote for the person you want to be.
