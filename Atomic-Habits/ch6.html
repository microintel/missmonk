PLAN 00 — the big idea

You don't need more willpower.
You need a better room.

This page explains one idea from the book Atomic Habits: your surroundings shape your habits more than your motivation does. Simple words. Real examples. Things you can try today.

PLAN 01 — why this works

You act on what you see, not what you plan

Walk into a kitchen and see cookies on the counter — you eat one, even if you weren't hungry. Walk past a bookshelf tucked in a corner — you never open a book, even if you meant to. Nothing about your willpower changed. Only what was in front of your eyes changed.

Out of all your senses, sight does the most work. Most of what your brain processes comes from your eyes. So a small change in what you see can cause a big change in what you do — without you even noticing it happening.

PLAN 02 — proof from a real hospital

The cafeteria that changed people's diets without saying a word

A doctor named Anne Thorndike ran a study in a hospital cafeteria. She didn't lecture anyone about eating healthy. She just moved things around: she added water next to the soda, and placed water baskets near every food counter. Soda stayed exactly where it was too. Nobody was told what to do.

Soda sales−11.4%
Bottled water sales+25.8%

Over three months, soda sales dropped and water sales rose — just because water became easier to grab. This is the whole idea in one experiment: change what's around you, and behaviour follows on its own.

PLAN 04 — one space, one job

A room can only teach you one habit at a time

If you scroll your phone in bed, watch TV at your study table, and eat snacks at your desk — every space starts to mean everything. Your brain gets confused about what each place is for.

Give each space one clear job. Over time, that space starts to trigger that one habit automatically.

Mixed-up room

  • Bed = sleeping + scrolling + snacking
  • Result: hard to fall asleep here
  • Desk = studying + gaming + eating
  • Result: hard to focus here

One-job room

  • Bed = only sleeping
  • Result: you feel sleepy the moment you lie down
  • Desk = only studying
  • Result: focus comes automatically

Even in a small room, you can copy this: one chair only for reading, one corner only for exercise. Small zones work just as well as separate rooms.

PLAN 05 — a fresh start helps

New places make new habits easier

It's hard to build a new habit where old habits already live. If you always watch videos on the couch, it's hard to suddenly "focus" on the same couch. A new place has no old cues pulling you back.

Try a new café to read in. A different park bench to think in. A different aisle order at the grocery store. A fresh space makes the new habit yours from the very first day.

PLAN 06 — what to do, what to avoid

In short

Do this

  • Put good cues in plain sight — water bottle on the desk, fruit bowl on the counter.
  • Give each room or corner just one job.
  • Move bad-habit objects out of sight and out of reach.
  • Use a new place when starting a hard new habit.
  • Add more than one cue for habits you really want to stick — a note, an object, a reminder.

Don't do this

  • Don't rely only on willpower or motivation — it runs out.
  • Don't hide the things you want to do more of (guitar, books, medicine).
  • Don't mix habits in one space (bed for scrolling AND sleeping).
  • Don't expect a new habit to stick in a room full of old temptations.
  • Don't blame yourself for "lack of discipline" before checking your environment first.
PLAN 07 — recap

Remember this

  • Small changes in your surroundings can lead to big changes in your habits over time.
  • Every habit starts with a cue. You notice cues that stand out — so make good cues loud and bad cues quiet.
  • Over time, a habit sticks not to one small cue, but to the whole space around it.
  • New habits are easier to build in a new place, because old cues aren't there to pull you back.
  • You're not stuck with your environment. You can design it.
