How to stop procrastinating with the Two-Minute Rule

Big goals feel heavy, so we never start. The fix isn't more willpower — it's shrinking the habit until it takes less than two minutes. Here's how that works, explained simply.

The Ritual

The habit isn't the workout. It's the taxi.

Twyla Tharp, a world-famous dancer, works out for two hours every morning. But she says her real habit is much smaller than that.

T
"The ritual is not the stretching and weight training. The ritual is the cab. The moment I tell the driver where to go, I have completed the ritual."
Twyla Tharp, choreographer

Same idea, everyday version: Vignesh wants to code for an hour every evening. His real habit isn't the hour of coding — it's opening his laptop and starting VS Code. Once that's done, the coding usually follows on its own.

Decisive Moments

A few small choices decide your whole day

Around 40–50% of what you do daily is pure habit — automatic, not decided in the moment. A handful of tiny choices, called decisive moments, quietly set the direction for hours afterward.

Try It

Do it for two minutes. Then stop.

That's the whole trick. You're not tricking yourself into doing more — the rule is simply to stop at two minutes. Most days, you'll want to keep going anyway.

Habit Shaping

Once showing up feels easy, scale up slowly

After the two-minute version becomes automatic, grow it in small phases. Dhanush wants to become an early riser for exam prep. Here's how he scales it up over weeks, not overnight.

In Practice

What to do, what to avoid

Do

  • Shrink any habit to under two minutes to start it
  • Focus on showing up, not on doing it perfectly
  • Stop on purpose at two minutes, even if you feel like continuing
  • Ritualize the first step (same time, same action) so it needs no thinking
  • Scale up gradually in small phases once showing up feels automatic

Don't

  • Start with the "final version" of the habit (1-hour gym, 3-hour study)
  • Judge yourself for doing "only" one page or one push-up
  • Skip the small version because it feels too easy to matter
  • Ignore decisive moments — they quietly steer your whole day
  • Try to optimize a habit before it even exists
Chapter Summary

The short version

  • A habit can take a few seconds, but it shapes what you do for the next hours.
  • Decisive moments are small forks in the road — one choice sends your day in a good or bad direction.
  • The Two-Minute Rule: any new habit should take less than two minutes to start.
  • Ritualizing the first step makes it easy to slip into focus and do the harder work that follows.
  • Standardize before you optimize — you can't improve a habit that doesn't exist yet.
