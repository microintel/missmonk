You don't lack motivation. You lack a clear plan for when and where.

Same motivation, same information — the only group that changed their behaviour is the one that got specific. That's the whole idea of this chapter.

Step 1

Say exactly when and where you'll do it

This is called an implementation intention. It's just a fancy name for a simple sentence — a plan you make before the moment arrives, so you never have to decide in the moment.

I will [BEHAVIOUR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION].

Step 2

Or hook it onto something you already do

This is called habit stacking. Instead of picking a clock time, you pick a habit you never skip — brushing your teeth, pouring your coffee, sitting down for dinner — and place the new habit right after it. The old habit becomes the reminder for the new one.

After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].

A quick story behind why this works: a poor writer named Diderot suddenly received a beautiful new robe as a gift. It made everything else he owned look shabby by comparison, so he replaced his rug, then his furniture, then his whole house — one upgrade pulling in the next. Habits chain together the same way. One action naturally cues the next, so if you slot a new habit right after an old one, it rides along for free.

Find your own cue

Two short lists help you find the right spot

Write down what you already do every day without fail, and what happens around you every day without fail. Your new habit fits best right next to one of these.

Things you already do

  • Get out of bed
  • Brush your teeth
  • Brew your coffee or tea
  • Leave for work or college
  • Sit down for dinner
  • Turn off the lights at night

Things that just happen

  • The sun rises
  • Your phone buzzes with a message
  • A song on your playlist ends
  • The sun sets
  • Your alarm rings
  • The bus or train arrives

Pick a cue that happens exactly as often as you want the new habit to happen. If you want a daily habit, don't hang it on something that only happens on Sundays.

Putting it together

What to do — and what to stop doing

Do this

  • Pick one exact time and one exact place for the habit
  • Attach new habits to old ones you never skip
  • Keep the cue small and specific — "after I close my laptop," not "sometime at lunch"
  • Start on a Monday, a new month, or a fresh day if you're unsure when to begin
  • Write your plan down somewhere you'll actually see it

Don't do this

  • Don't say "I'll exercise more" without a day, time or place
  • Don't wait to "feel motivated" before you start
  • Don't stack a daily habit onto a weekly one
  • Don't pick a busy, chaotic moment as your cue
  • Don't leave the habit as one vague idea in your head
Chapter 5 in one breath

The 1st law of behaviour change is make it obvious. Time and location are the two most powerful cues. An implementation intention gives a habit a fixed time and place. Habit stacking gives it a fixed spot in a routine you already run every day.
