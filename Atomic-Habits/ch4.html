The Man Who Didn't Look Right

Your brain runs most of your day without asking you first. Here's how that happens, why it can help or hurt you, and the simple trick that puts you back in charge — explained the easy way.

The Story

She Couldn't Explain It. She Just Knew.

A paramedic walked into a family party and looked at her father-in-law. Something felt wrong. She couldn't say what — just that his face looked "off." She told him to go to the hospital right away.

A few hours later, doctors found a blocked artery. He was minutes away from a heart attack. Her years of seeing sick patients had trained her brain to notice a pattern — a change in the colour of blood flow near the skin — without her even realising she was looking for it.

Your brain is a prediction machine. It quietly studies everything you go through, and over time it learns to notice things you never consciously taught it to notice. — the same reason radiologists spot strokes early, and hairdressers can guess a client is pregnant just by touching her hair.

This is amazing when it works for you. But it's the exact same system that makes bad habits run on autopilot too.

When Autopilot Backfires

Three People, Three Habits They Didn't Notice

Once a habit is deep enough, it fires on its own — even when it makes no sense. Meet Vignesh, Pranav and Dhanush.

Vignesh — the shop clerk

Vignesh worked at a mobile recharge shop where he was trained to cut up every expired top-up card with scissors before handing back change. One evening, a regular customer paid with his own debit card. Vignesh took it, picked up the scissors out of pure habit, and snipped it in half — before he even realised what his hands had done.

Pranav — the ex-teacher

Pranav taught primary school for eight years, always reminding kids to wash their hands. He later switched to an IT job. Months in, he still catches himself asking his teammates, completely on reflex, "Did you wash your hands?" — right after they return from a lunch break.

Dhanush — the lifeguard

Dhanush spent his college years as a swimming pool lifeguard, shouting "Walk!" at running kids. Now he works in an office. Whenever a colleague jogs down the corridor, some part of him still shouts "Walk!" before he can stop himself.

None of them chose to do this in the moment. The habit was already running before conscious thought caught up. This is exactly why the book says: if a habit stays unconscious, you can never improve it.

The Fix, Part 1

Say It Out Loud — "Point and Call"

Japanese train drivers physically point at the signal, the speed gauge and the clock, and say out loud what they see: "Signal is green." "Speed, 40." It looks silly. It works because it forces the automatic brain to become conscious again, just for a second — long enough to catch mistakes before they happen.

The results, once this was measured on real railway systems:

Fewer errors overall
85%
Fewer accidents
30%
Fewer wrongly-parked trains (NYC subway)
57%

You can do the same at home. Next time you reach for a cigarette or a fourth samosa, say out loud: "I'm about to eat this, but I don't need it — it won't help my health." Hearing yourself say it makes the choice feel real again.

The Fix, Part 2

Try It: Your Own Habits Scorecard

List out your daily actions and mark each one honestly: + for a good habit, − for a bad one, = for neutral. There's no need to change anything yet — just notice. Tap the buttons below to try it on a sample day, or add your own.

Not sure how to mark something? Ask yourself: "Does this habit help me become the person I want to be?" If yes, it's a +. If it works against that, it's a −.

Takeaway

What To Do — and What Not To Do

Do this

  • Notice your habits first, before trying to fix anything.
  • Say your next action out loud when you want to catch a bad habit ("Point and Call").
  • Make a simple Habits Scorecard of your normal day.
  • Ask "does this cast a vote for the person I want to be?"
  • Watch yourself calmly, like you're observing someone else.

Don't do this

  • Don't blame or criticise yourself while noticing a bad habit — just observe it.
  • Don't try to fix every habit at once. Awareness comes first, change comes later.
  • Don't assume small automatic actions don't matter — they add up over months.
  • Don't skip the awareness step and jump straight to "willpower."
  • Don't ignore a habit just because you can't explain why you do it.
Chapter Summary

In Short

  • With enough repetition, your brain learns to notice cues on its own, without you thinking about it.
  • Once a habit becomes automatic, you stop paying attention to it — like Vignesh, Pranav and Dhanush.
  • Real change always starts with awareness. You can't fix what you don't notice.
  • "Point and Call" — saying your action out loud — brings a sleeping habit back into conscious view.
  • The Habits Scorecard is a simple way to see your whole day clearly, one habit at a time.
