Atomic Habits · Chapter 14

Make good habits inevitable. Make bad habits impossible.

You don't always need more willpower. Sometimes you just need to lock the door so temptation can't walk in. This chapter shows you how — with real stories, one demo you can try, and one chart that explains why automation beats motivation.

The story

Victor Hugo locked away his own clothes

In 1830, the writer Victor Hugo was a year late on a book. His publisher gave him six months. Hugo's fix was strange but brilliant: he had an assistant lock away all his clothes, leaving him only a shawl. With nothing to wear outside, he stayed in and wrote. The Hunchback of Notre Dame was finished two weeks early.

Hugo didn't try to become more disciplined. He made the bad habit — going out instead of writing — physically impossible. Psychologists call this a commitment device: a choice you make today that controls what you can do tomorrow.

P
Pranav's version of Hugo's trick

Pranav kept opening his trading app during work hours and making impulsive trades. So he did his own "lock the clothes" move — he removed saved login details and turned on a 30-minute app-lock timer. Now checking the market takes real effort, not one tap. He still trades, just not on impulse.

The story

The cash register that stopped stealing overnight

John Patterson owned a small supply store in Ohio. It should have made money, but it didn't — his own employees were stealing from the open cash drawer. He bought two of the newly invented cash registers, which locked the cash and receipts after every sale.

Theft stopped that same week. In six months, his store went from losing money to earning the equivalent of over ₹80 lakh in profit today. He didn't lecture his staff on honesty — he made stealing impossible with a machine.

The best way to break a bad habit is to make it impractical to do.

This is the real power move: don't just make bad habits harder — automate the good behavior so it happens without anyone deciding anything.

D
Dhanush automates his savings

Dhanush kept meaning to save money but always "forgot" by month-end. He set up an auto-debit of ₹2,000 to a separate savings account the day his salary lands. He never has to decide to save again — it just happens, like Patterson's cash register.

Why this works

Willpower fades. Automation doesn't.

If you rely on motivation alone, the effort you need to keep a habit going stays high forever — and some days you simply won't have it. Automate the habit once, and the ongoing effort drops toward zero.

The other side

Automation can work against you too

Technology cuts both ways. Auto-refilling your medicine is automation helping you. Auto-playing the next video is automation working against you — because stopping now takes more effort than continuing.

Chapter summary

What to do, what to avoid

Do this

  • Create a commitment device for your worst habit — lock the "clothes," not just your resolve.
  • Find one onetime setup (savings auto-debit, app deletion) that pays off without daily effort.
  • Let technology automate good behaviour: auto bill-pay, auto refills, auto savings.
  • Make the bad habit take more steps than the good one.

Avoid this

  • Don't rely only on willpower for habits you repeat every single day — it runs out.
  • Don't leave autoplay, saved cards, and one-tap ordering switched on "by default."
  • Don't treat "I'll just have a little" as a real plan — Hugo didn't trust himself either.
  • Don't automate blindly — check occasionally that the automation still serves you.
