Step 1 — Where habits come from

A cat, a box, and a lever

In 1898, a psychologist named Edward Thorndike put a cat inside a box. The only way out was to press a hidden lever. The first time, the cat had no idea what to do. It scratched, pushed, clawed at everything — pure trial and error.

But something changed every time the cat got out. Watch the cat's escape time drop, trial after trial:

The cat wasn't getting smarter. It was building a habit — a mental shortcut for a problem it kept facing. That's the whole chapter in one sentence: a habit is a behaviour repeated so often it becomes automatic.

Step 2 — Why your brain even bothers

Habits are just your brain saving effort

Your conscious mind can only focus on one thing properly at a time. So your brain hands off anything repetitive to autopilot. That's a habit. It's not laziness — it's efficiency.

Vignesh used to think hard every single morning about which route to take to college. After two months of the same route, he doesn't think about it at all — his hands just steer the bike. His brain freed up that thinking space for something else, like revising for his exam.

This is also why "habits kill freedom" is a myth. It's the opposite. Pranav, who has a fixed habit of packing his bag the night before, never scrambles in the morning — that habit gives him 15 calm extra minutes, not less freedom.

Last step — the simple version

What to do, and what not to do

No theory this time — just the plain rules from this chapter.

Do this
  • Notice the habits you already run on autopilot — most of your day is already habits, not decisions.
  • Make the cue for a good habit obvious. Dhanush keeps his gym shoes by the door, not in the cupboard.
  • Attach a small, real reward to the habit, so your brain wants to repeat it.
  • Start stupidly small. Two minutes of reading beats zero minutes of "I'll do it properly later."
  • Judge yourself by the system you follow daily, not one big goal far away.
Don't do this
  • Don't rely on willpower alone — Thorndike's cat didn't have willpower, it had repetition. So do you.
  • Don't hide the cue for a bad habit in plain sight. If chips are on the table, you will eat them.
  • Don't chase huge, effortful first steps. High friction kills a new habit before it starts.
  • Don't skip the "reward" step. A habit with no satisfaction attached will not survive.
  • Don't believe "I just need more motivation." Fix the cue and the friction first.
