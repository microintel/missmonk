The Story

A father who believed genius is built, not born

In 1965, a man named Laszlo Polgar decided to test one idea: that with the right habits and the right home, any child can become brilliant at anything. He picked chess. He filled the house with chess books, hung pictures of champions on the wall, and made playing chess the family's daily normal.

His three daughters grew up surrounded by chess — so chess is what they loved. One daughter became a grandmaster. The youngest, Judit, became the youngest grandmaster in history at the time, beating a record held by Bobby Fischer.

"Sofia, leave the pieces alone!" her father said one night.
"Daddy, they won't leave me alone!" she replied.

The sisters didn't force themselves to practice. Chess simply felt normal — because everyone around them treated it as normal. That's the whole chapter in one story.

Why This Happens

Fitting in used to mean staying alive

For most of human history, being kicked out of your group was dangerous. So our brains still treat "belonging" as a survival need. This is why a habit feels easy the moment people around you already do it — and feels hard the moment it makes you stand out from them.

Real example: in one long study, a person's chance of gaining weight went up by 57% if a close friend gained weight too. Habits — good or bad — spread through people, almost like a cold.

Three Groups We Copy

Close. Many. Powerful.

We don't copy everyone equally. We mainly copy three kinds of people — and once you notice this, you can use it on purpose.

01

The Close

People we spend the most time with — family, best friends, roommates.

Vignesh starts going to bed at 10pm because his roommate does it every night. After a month, it just feels normal — he didn't "decide" it, he absorbed it.
02

The Many

What most people around us are doing — the crowd, the office, the neighbourhood.

Pranav joins a running club where everyone runs every Sunday. He never runs alone at home, but at the club he shows up every week — because that's just what the group does.
03

The Powerful

People with status or success we look up to and quietly imitate.

Dhanush notices his top-performing colleague reaches office at 8am and reads every morning. Without anyone telling him to, Dhanush starts doing the same — he wants what that respect looks like.

Putting It To Use

What to do — and what to avoid

✓ Do this

  • Spend time near people who already do what you want to become. If Vignesh wants to read more, he should sit with friends who read, not just tell himself to try harder.
  • Join a group where the habit is already "normal" — a gym batch, a study group, a walking club.
  • Pick a group you already feel similar to (same interest, same background) — change feels easier when it feels like "people like me already do this."
  • Notice who you admire, and copy their small daily habits, not just their success.

✗ Avoid this

  • Don't assume willpower alone will fix a habit if your daily circle works against it. If Pranav's friends eat out every night, his diet plan alone won't be enough.
  • Don't stay quiet and go along with a group just to avoid disagreement — that's how the wrong habits spread too.
  • Don't underestimate quiet pressure from people close to you — it works even when nobody says a word.
  • Don't try to change completely alone if a supportive group is available — it's much harder than it needs to be.
