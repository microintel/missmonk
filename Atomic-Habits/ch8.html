Supernormal Stimuli

Vignesh and the three-dot beak

Vignesh writes code for a living and tracks his money closely. His phone's stock ticker isn't a plain number — it's red and green, pulsing, refreshing every second, with alerts that buzz his pocket. That's not an accident. It's an exaggerated cue, built to be more attractive than a real market ever needs to be.

Nature's version

Three red dots on a beak

Herring gull chicks peck harder at a fake beak with three oversized red dots than at their real parent. The exaggerated cue overrides the real one.

Vignesh's version

A ticker that never stops

Live colour-coded prices, push alerts, and a red/green flash on every tick. His brain treats each flash like a red dot — worth checking, right now.

Everyone's version

The "like" counter

A notification badge, a slot-machine pull, a bag of chips engineered for the "bliss point" — all heightened versions of a normal reward.

The Real Engine

It's not the reward. It's the anticipation.

Dopamine doesn't spike when Pranav sees his portfolio go up. It spikes a second before — the moment he opens the app, expecting good news. Wanting and liking are two different systems, and wanting is by far the bigger one.

Your brain has a huge "want" engine, and a tiny "like" engine

The nucleus accumbens — a key reward hub — is almost entirely devoted to wanting. Only a sliver of it handles actual liking.

Why it works

This uses Premack's Principle: a behaviour you already do a lot (watching Netflix) can reinforce a behaviour you rarely do (cycling). Over time, the two feel like one action.

The formula from the book:

  1. After [current habit], I will do [habit I need].
  2. After [habit I need], I get to do [habit I want].

Chapter 8 Summary

What to do — and what to avoid

Do

  • Pair a want with a need. Bundle a habit you're avoiding with one you already crave.
  • Notice the anticipation. The urge before an action is the real driver — use it on purpose.
  • Make good habits visually rewarding. A streak, a chart, a small ritual — give your brain something to look forward to.
  • Question your cues. Ask why an app or food feels irresistible before assuming it's just "willpower."

Don't

  • Don't confuse wanting with liking. Craving a habit doesn't mean it's still making you happy.
  • Don't chase every supernormal stimulus. Junk food, endless feeds, and hyper-designed apps are built to be over-attractive, not good for you.
  • Don't skip the "need" step. If the want comes first, the bundle stops working.
  • Don't rely on willpower alone. Redesign the cue instead of fighting the craving head-on.
