The Vietnam Discovery

Same addiction. Two completely different endings.

In 1971, researchers found that a shocking number of U.S. soldiers in Vietnam were addicted to heroin. Everyone expected them to relapse once home. Instead, almost all of them quit — nearly overnight. Nothing about their willpower changed. Their surroundings did.

The addiction didn't vanish because the soldiers became stronger people. It vanished because every cue that triggered the habit — easy access, war stress, addicted friends, being far from home — disappeared the moment they landed back in the US.
Old environment

Same neighbourhood, same rehab story

A typical user gets clean in a clinic, then goes straight back to the same street, same friends, same triggers.

  • Same people who use around them
  • Same shop, same route, same room
  • Every cue still switched "on"
New environment

Vietnam soldiers, back home

Different city, different friends, no war stress, no easy access. The habit had nothing left to attach to.

  • No familiar triggers around
  • No cue-carrying friend circle
  • The context changed, so the habit changed
Why This Happens In The Brain

A habit is never really forgotten. It just goes quiet.

Once a habit is carved into the brain, the urge to act comes back the instant the old cue reappears — even years later. Scientists showed addicts a photo of cocaine for just 33 milliseconds — far too fast to consciously notice — and their brain's reward pathway still lit up. They wanted it before they knew they'd seen it.

33 ms

is all it takes for a single glimpse of a cue to spark craving — before your conscious mind even registers what it saw. This is why bad habits are autocatalytic: the habit feeds the feeling it's supposed to numb, in an endless loop.

The Real Difference

"Disciplined" people aren't stronger. They're just less exposed.

When scientists studied people with great self-control, they weren't superhuman at resisting urges — they simply spent far less of their day inside tempting situations in the first place. Less exposure means less willpower is ever needed.

The Fix — Inversion of the 1st Law

Make it obvious → Make it invisible

You can't out-willpower a bad habit forever. The far easier move is to remove the cue that starts it. One small change, and the whole habit often fades on its own.

Chapter Summary

What to do — and what to avoid

Do this
  • Fill out a Habits Scorecard — write down your daily habits so you notice them.
  • Remove the cue that triggers a bad habit, instead of just resisting it.
  • Design your space so good habits are the easy, obvious option.
  • Accept that a habit never fully disappears — plan your environment around that.
  • Spend energy on your surroundings, not on fighting temptation daily.
Don't do this
  • Don't rely on willpower alone — it runs out fast, every single day.
  • Don't blame yourself as "lacking discipline" — it's usually the environment.
  • Don't return to the exact same triggers and expect a different result.
  • Don't use shame or scare-tactics on yourself — stress often triggers the very habit you're fighting.
  • Don't keep tempting cues "just in sight" — out of sight really is out of mind.
