Atomic Habits · Chapter 10

Your habits are not random.
They are predictions.

Every habit — good or bad — starts the same way: your brain sees a cue, guesses what will happen, and feels a craving. Learn how this loop works, using three simple examples, and what to actually do about it.

Vignesh Pranav Dhanush

01

A dinner in Istanbul

James Clear (the author) once had dinner with seven people in Turkey. He was the only one who had never smoked. Half the others had quit — and most of them quit using the same trick: they changed the way they thought about smoking.

One friend told him: "Stop lying to yourself. You don't actually enjoy this. You don't need it." Once he stopped seeing smoking as a reward, he stopped wanting it.

This is the big idea of this chapter: you don't crave the habit itself — you crave the feeling you expect it will give you. Change what you expect, and you change the craving.

04

Change the prediction, change the craving

You cannot delete a craving. But you can change what a cue predicts. Try flipping "have to" into "get to" — same task, new meaning. Pick a name to see it in action.

Situation: Vignesh has to save money instead of buying new sneakers every month.

Old thought

"I have to stop spending. It feels like giving something up."

New thought

"I get to keep more of my own money. Every rupee saved this month buys me more freedom next month."

Situation: Pranav wants to stop reaching for a cigarette when he's stressed.

Old thought

"Smoking calms my nerves. I need it to relax."

New thought

"Smoking doesn't calm me — it's the only reason I feel anxious in the first place. Without it, there's nothing to relieve."

Situation: Dhanush dreads his 6 AM run.

Old thought

"I need to go run. It's tiring and I lose an hour of sleep."

New thought

"It's time to build endurance and get fast. Every run makes tomorrow's version of me stronger."

05

Build a "motivation ritual"

Do something small you enjoy right before a hard habit. Repeat it enough times, and that small enjoyable thing becomes the on-switch for feeling ready.

How many times each person repeated their ritual before it "clicked" and started working automatically:

Vignesh
12 times
Pranav
18 times
Dhanush
9 times

Example: Dhanush puts on his running shoes and stretches for 2 minutes every morning before his run — even on days he doesn't feel like running. After about 9 days, just tying his shoelaces started to put him in "run mode."

06

What to do, and what not to do

✓ Do this

  • Ask "what am I really trying to feel right now?" before acting on a craving.
  • Rewrite "I have to" as "I get to" for one hard task today.
  • Link a habit you enjoy right before a habit you find hard (e.g. music before a workout).
  • Write down the real benefits of quitting a bad habit — health, money, time, respect.
  • Notice the cue that triggers a bad habit, not just the habit itself.

✗ Don't do this

  • Don't rely only on willpower — it fades faster than a changed belief.
  • Don't tell yourself a habit is "impossible to quit" — that belief itself keeps it alive.
  • Don't ignore the environment — the same cue almost always leads to the same craving.
  • Don't expect one reframe to work instantly — it takes repeats to feel automatic.
  • Don't confuse the habit with the need — chasing the wrong habit never satisfies the real need.
07

Cheat sheet: the 4 laws

This chapter is part of Law 2. Here's how all 4 laws work, for building good habits and breaking bad ones.

1
Make it obvious
Notice your habits. Say out loud when and where you'll do it. Put cues where you can see them.
Break it: make it invisible — hide the cue.
2
Make it attractive
Pair it with something you enjoy. Join people who already do it. Reframe the meaning.
Break it: make it unattractive — list the real downsides.
3
Make it easy
Reduce the steps between you and the habit.
Break it: make it difficult — add friction.
4
Make it satisfying
Give yourself an immediate small reward.
Break it: make it unsatisfying — add an immediate cost.
The takeaway

A craving is just a feeling you expect a habit to give you. Change the expectation, and the craving changes with it.
