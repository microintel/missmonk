💰

Income Statement

Lists all the sales and expenses incurred by the company during the year, resulting in net profit or loss. Prepared annually (12 months) or quarterly (3 months).

⚖️

Balance Sheet

Lists all that the company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a given point in time — specifically at 31st March of every year in India.

🔄

Cash Flow Statement

Tracks every rupee entering and leaving the company during the year. Unlike the income statement, this only records actual cash movements.

Every income statement is structured in three parts — Revenue, Expenses, and the resulting Profit or Loss:

💚 Revenue Side

  • Revenue from operations (core business)
  • Other revenue (interest, asset gains)
  • Break-up from notes to accounts

❤️ Expense Side

  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
  • Employee benefit expenses
  • Finance costs (interest)
  • Depreciation & Amortisation
  • Other expenses

💛 Profit / Loss

  • Profit/Loss before taxes
  • Other comprehensive income
  • Tax deductions
  • Net Profit after tax
  • EPS (Earnings Per Share)

Understanding EPS — Earnings Per Share

EPS  =  Net Profit Total Shares Outstanding
Example: ₹200 Cr. ÷ 5 Cr. shares = ₹40 per share

Real data reference: Godawari Power & Ispat Ltd Annual Report FY21 — Net Profit after tax: ₹604 Crores

Section One, Continued

The Balance Sheet

A balance sheet lists a company's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a specific point in time — typically 31st March every year in India.

Unlike the income statement which covers a period, the balance sheet is a snapshot in time. Think of it like a photograph of the company's financial health on a single day.

Assets

A resource which a company owns and controls, with the ability to generate future economic benefit.

Cash Land Machinery Automobiles Inventory Short-term Investments Goodwill

Liabilities

An obligation which a company owes and will have to transfer economic benefit to some external party.

Bank Loans Trade Payables Borrowings Provisions Loans from Suppliers
Total Assets  =  Total Liabilities + Equity   ·   Both sides MUST balance — always.
📦 Long-Term Assets
  • Fixed Assets & work in progress
  • Long-term Investments
  • Intangible assets
  • Goodwill
⚡ Short-Term Assets
  • Inventory
  • Account Receivables
  • Cash & Cash Equivalents
  • Short-term Investments
  • Other current assets
👥 Equity
  • Shareholder Capital
  • Retained Earnings
  • Reserves & Surplus
  • Profit/Loss after taxes
🔴 Long-Term Liabilities
  • Long-term Borrowings
  • Deferred Tax Liabilities
  • Provisions
  • Other long-term liabilities
🟣 Short-Term Liabilities
  • Accounts Payable
  • Short-term Borrowings
  • Other short-term liabilities

4.2
Section Two

Cash Flow
Statement

The cash flow statement is a list of all actual cash inflows and outflows for the business from April 1st to March 31st (the accounting year). It is fundamentally different from the income statement.

Key Distinction: Imagine your company sold goods worth ₹10 Crores on 3-month credit. The income statement will record this as ₹10 Cr. in sales immediately. But since no cash has changed hands yet — it will NOT appear in the cash flow statement. The cash flow only moves when money actually moves.
⚙️ Cash Flow from Operations
  • All cash from day-to-day business activity
  • Working Capital Changes
  • Direct taxes paid
  • Net cash flow from/used in operating activities
  • Example: Electricity sales receipts, expense payments
📈 Cash Flow from Investing
  • Purchase and sale of fixed assets
  • Purchase and sale of financial assets
  • Interest received on investments
  • Any long-term asset purchase = investing activity
🏦 Cash Flow from Financing
  • Equity issued or bought back
  • Proceeds from new debt issuance
  • Capital used for debt repayment
  • Interest / Dividends paid
📊 Net Cash Flow Change
  • Sum of all three components above
  • Added to previous year's cash balance
  • Gives current year-end cash position
  • Cash and cash equivalents at year end
↓ INFLOW

Cash Inflow

Includes all cash received through sales, advance payments, and other sources. Debt financing by the company is also counted as cash inflow. Examples: Raising capital, selling machines.

↑ OUTFLOW

Cash Outflow

Includes dividends paid and expenses incurred by the company, or when money leaves the company due to any other reason — purchasing assets, repaying loans, etc.

How Net Cash Position is Calculated

Net Cash Flow (Current Year) – ₹60 Cr.
Cash Balance from Previous Year + ₹200 Cr.
Cash on Hand (Year End) ₹140 Cr.

This is essentially how a cash flow statement is constructed. It tells us how management is using cash, and places a lot of emphasis on how cash is being utilized within the company.

Three Statements,
One Story

We've now covered all three core financial statements that every stock investor must understand. Together, they paint a complete picture of a company's financial health.

There is also a fourth statement — the Statement of Changes in Equity — which reveals if the company has issued or repurchased any equity, or altered its ownership structure.

Income Statement → What did the company earn?
Balance Sheet → What does it own & owe right now?
Cash Flow → How is it actually managing its cash?

At first, these statements may feel overwhelming. But with practice, finding and analyzing information becomes intuitive. Now go read as many annual reports as you can!
