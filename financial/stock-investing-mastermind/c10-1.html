How to Analyze aCompany

A 4-pillar framework to deeply evaluate any stock before you invest your money — from products to management.

Products & Services Industry Research Business Research Management
6.1

The 4-Pillar Framework

Every stock analysis starts here — these four lenses tell you everything you need to know.

01 ·

Products & Services

What does the company sell? Who buys it? Why do they buy it? Is there anything special about it?

02 ·

Industry Research

How big is the market? Is it stable? Are other players doing well? What's the future outlook?

03 ·

Business Research

Where does the company stand in its industry? Is it a market leader or a niche player?

04 ·

Management

Who runs the company? Are they qualified? How are they paid? Do their interests align with shareholders?

Key principle: We will also study financial performance, different ratio types, and KPIs for each sector. We'll identify who owns the stock, if smart money follows the firm, and what the ownership trend looks like. Think of this chapter as your complete investor's due diligence checklist.
6.2

Products & Services

Understand what the company sells before anything else.

🔍

Features of the Product

What are the product's core characteristics? What problem does it solve? Is it sticky or one-time?

👥

Who Uses It & Why?

Identify the exact target audience — age, gender, income group — and their core reason for buying.

⭐

Uniqueness / USP

What's the unique selling proposition? Brand trust, lower cost, distribution strength, or better quality?

📊

Similar Products

Compile a full list of competitors. Who else sells this? Is the company the leader or a follower?

🔄

Available Substitutes

Can the product be completely replaced? EVs replace petrol cars. Pepsi can replace Coke. Does this company face such a risk?

📁

Data Sources

Company websites, investor presentations, YouTube demos, product brochures, Annual Reports (MD&A section).

Substitution Decision Tree
STEP 01 ·

Is there a substitute?

Can the entire product category be replaced? EV → Petrol cars. Digital → Print.

STEP 02 ·

Are people actually switching?

Existence of substitute ≠ adoption. Check if real customers are moving away.

STEP 03 ·

What's the advantage?

Cost? Convenience? Performance? Understand the reason behind switching behavior.

VERDICT ·

No substitute? Invest with confidence.

If there's no real substitute and people keep buying, that's a durable business.

Maggi Case Study: In the noodles market, multiple brands compete — yet Maggi dominates. Why? It's not just one thing. Distribution Network + Brand Recognition = Maggi's popularity. The product reaches even the smallest kirana stores across PAN India. This is called a distribution moat — harder to replicate than product features alone.

Health of Players — What It Signals
ALL DOING WELL

Healthy Industry ✓

When all/most companies in the sector show good performance — like Marico, Dabur, Trichup, Indulekha all growing — the industry itself has strong tailwinds.

MOST STRUGGLING

Poor Industry ✗

If all except one player is doing poorly, it suggests the whole industry is struggling — not just one bad company. Avoid this sector.

Golden Rule: An average performer in a healthy industry beats a great performer in a sick industry. Jio is great — but the entire telecom industry has been price-war-stressed for years.
Industry Outlook — What to Evaluate
⚠ Major Risk Example — EV vs Petroleum

If EVs are expected to cover 40% of the market in 10 years, the petroleum distribution industry is at risk. Petrol pump companies may struggle. This is a structural threat — avoid investing in that sector even if the top company looks profitable today.

✓ Growth Outlook Example — Tech & Digital

Products and services moving to digital platforms. Tech, SaaS, digital fintech — all have positive structural tailwinds. Consumer behavior is shifting toward digital, not away. Invest in industries where the wind is at your back.

6.4

Business Research

Where does the company sit within its industry?

🏆

Market Position

Is the company a market leader, challenger, or niche player? Leaders like Marico in hair oil have pricing power and brand loyalty that smaller players can't match.

🛡️

Competitive Moat

What prevents competitors from stealing customers? Brand (Marico), distribution (Maggi), patents, or switching costs (B2B industrial like APAR)?

📍

Market Share Trend

Is the company growing or losing share? Asian Paints holds ~50% of the paint industry — any further growth requires either industry expansion or entering new segments.

The Asian Paints Lesson: When a company holds 50% market share in a ₹62,000 crore industry, it can only grow further by (1) the industry itself expanding, or (2) entering new geographies/product lines. No company can grow bigger than its own industry. This is why market size matters as a ceiling for growth.
Behaviour Shifts — The Hidden Risk
STABLE BEHAVIOUR ✓

Hair Oil Industry

People's hair care habits don't change drastically. Marico and Dabur have led for 15+ years. Behaviour is predictable → safe to invest.

SHIFTING BEHAVIOUR ✗

Social Media Platforms

Facebook → Instagram → TikTok → ? Platforms that were dominant 5 years ago have lost younger audiences rapidly. Risky for long-term investors.

6.5

Management Background

The people running the company are as important as the company itself.

🎓 Qualifications & Track Record

  • What is the CEO/founders' background and education?
  • Have they successfully run a company before?
  • Do they have relevant domain experience in this industry?
  • How long have they been with the company?

💰 Compensation Structure

  • What is the salary vs performance bonus ratio?
  • Is executive pay linked to shareholder returns?
  • Are they paid too much relative to the company size?
  • ESOP (employee stock options) — do they eat their own cooking?

📌 Ownership & Vested Interests

  • How much stock does the promoter hold? (High = good alignment)
  • Is promoter holding increasing or decreasing?
  • Is smart institutional money (FIIs, DIIs) following the stock?
  • Check ownership trend — promoters selling is a red flag.

🗣️ Communication & Integrity

  • Do they give honest guidance — even bad news?
  • Read old investor calls: did they deliver on past promises?
  • Management that only paints rosy pictures → be cautious.
  • Prioritize industry reports over management statements for outlook.
Smart Investor Tip: A company's management will always present a positive outlook. So don't rely only on their statements. Cross-reference with independent industry reports. And always check the ownership trend — if promoters are slowly reducing their stake, that's a silent red flag no press release will mention.
