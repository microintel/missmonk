Reading the Balance Sheet

What is a Balance Sheet?

Solvency

Can the company pay its debts in the long run?

Solvent Company
Assets > Liabilities
Positive net worth. Can repay debts.
Insolvent Company
Assets < Liabilities
May face bankruptcy procedures.
Solvency ≠ Liquidity. A company can own land worth ₹100 Cr (solvent) but have zero cash to pay salaries this week (illiquid). Solvency = long-term. Liquidity = short-term.

1.1 Debt to Equity Ratio

Measures how much of the company is funded by debt vs. equity. Low D/E = company relies more on its own money. High D/E = relies heavily on borrowed money, which is risky.

D/E Ratio = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity
Apar Industries FY21:
Debt = ₹191 Cr (non-current) + ₹21 Cr (current) = ₹212 Cr
Equity = ₹1,409 Cr
D/E = 212 ÷ 1409 = 0.13
✓ D/E = 0.13 — Debt is only 13% of equity
🟢 Interpretation: Apar is primarily funded by equity. It can easily repay all debt. This shows very high solvency and very low financial risk.

1.2 Interest Coverage Ratio

Can the company's earnings comfortably cover the interest it owes? If EBIT = ₹700 Cr and interest = ₹100 Cr, coverage is 7x — very safe. If EBIT = ₹150 Cr and interest = ₹100 Cr, coverage is only 1.5x — dangerous.

Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
where EBIT = PBT + Interest (Finance Cost)
PBT = ₹208 Cr
Interest = ₹136 Cr
EBIT = 208 + 136 = ₹344 Cr
ICR = 344 ÷ 136 = 2.5×
ICR = 2.5× — interest is covered 2.5 times over
🟡 Interpretation: ICR of 2.5x is fair but not spectacular. A ratio above 3x is considered comfortable. For context, Apar's real business strength comes from low D/E rather than high ICR.
Safe Zone
ICR > 3×
Earnings comfortably cover interest. Low default risk.
Danger Zone
ICR < 1.5×
One bad quarter and debt repayment is at risk.

Liquidity

Can the company cover short-term bills?

Liquidity vs. Solvency — the key difference

Solvency asks: will the company survive long-term? Liquidity asks: can it pay its bills this month? A company can be perfectly solvent but temporarily illiquid if its assets are locked in land or inventory.

Current Ratio

Compares current (short-term) assets to current liabilities. A ratio above 1 means the company can pay all short-term bills today.

Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Current Assets = ₹4,042 Cr
Current Liabilities = ₹3,320 Cr
Current Ratio = 4042 ÷ 3320 = 1.2×
Current Ratio = 1.2× — current assets exceed liabilities

How to interpret Current Ratio

RatioMeaningSignal
Below 1.0Current liabilities exceed assets⚠️ Danger
1.0 – 1.5Just enough coverage🟡 Adequate
1.5 – 2.5Good liquidity buffer✅ Healthy
Above 3.0Very safe but may signal idle cashℹ️ Review
🟡 Apar at 1.2× is adequate. It's a manufacturing business so some capital is tied up in inventory — this is normal.

Quick Ratio (Acid Test)

A stricter test — removes inventory from current assets because inventory takes time to sell. Quick assets = Cash + Marketable Securities + Accounts Receivable.

Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities
= Quick Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Apar Industries:
Quick Assets = ₹4,042 − ₹1,562 = ₹2,480 Cr
Quick Ratio = 2480 ÷ 3320 = ~0.75×
🟡 Quick ratio below 1 is common for manufacturing companies with large inventories. It means the company depends on selling its inventory to cover short-term dues — which adds some risk, but is normal for the industry.

Efficiency

How well does the company use its assets?

Total Assets Turnover

Revenue generated per rupee of assets. Higher = more efficient. An asset-light company (software, consulting) generates massive revenue on few assets. A capital-heavy company (steel, manufacturing) generates less per rupee of assets — but that's not necessarily bad.

Total Assets Turnover = Revenue ÷ Total Assets
Revenue = ₹6,410 Cr
Total Assets = ₹5,008 Cr
Turnover = 6410 ÷ 5008 = 1.3×
For every ₹1 of assets, Apar generates ₹1.30 of revenue
Asset-light (e.g. TCS)
~3–8×
Minimal capital needed for high revenue
Asset-heavy (e.g. Steel)
~0.5–1.5×
Large capital tied in plant/machinery
⚠️ Always compare within the same industry. A 1.3× asset turnover for a cable/conductor manufacturer is healthy — but low for an IT company.

Working Capital Turnover

How efficiently is the net short-term capital (current assets minus current liabilities) being used to generate revenue?

Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
WC Turnover = Revenue ÷ Working Capital
Working Capital = ₹4,042 − ₹3,320 = ₹722 Cr
WC Turnover = 6410 ÷ 722 = ~9×
Apar turns its working capital into revenue ~9 times a year
🟢 A working capital turnover of 9× means the company operates with very lean short-term capital — it gets a lot of revenue out of a small working capital base. This is a sign of operational efficiency.

Day Sales Outstanding (DSO)

How many days does it take to collect payment after a sale? Lower is better — it means customers pay faster. High DSO ties up cash in receivables.

Receivables Turnover = Revenue ÷ Trade Receivables
DSO = 365 ÷ Receivables Turnover
Receivables Turnover = 6410 ÷ 1861 = 3.6×
DSO = 365 ÷ 3.6 = ~100 days
Apar collects payment in ~100 days on average
🟡 100 days is on the higher side but common for industrial/B2B businesses where large buyers demand credit terms. Monitor this — rising DSO over time could signal collection problems.

Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)

How many days of inventory is held at any point? Lower = inventory sells faster.

Inventory Turnover = Revenue ÷ Inventory
DIO = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover
Inventory Turnover = 6410 ÷ 1562 = 4.1×
DIO = 365 ÷ 4.1 = ~89 days

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

The total number of days it takes to convert investments in inventory into cash from sales. It combines DSO + DIO − Payable Period.

CCC = DSO + DIO − Payable Period (DPO)

Gross Profit Margin

The markup over direct production cost (COGS). It shows the core pricing power of the business before operating costs.

Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue
= (6410 − 4800) ÷ 6410 = 1610 ÷ 6410 = 25%
₹25 of every ₹100 revenue remains after production cost
🟢 25% gross margin for a manufacturing company (cables/conductors) is reasonable. The majority of cost is raw material — typical for commodity-intensive businesses.

EBIT Margin (Operating Margin)

After paying all operating costs including depreciation — what's left? This is the real operating profitability before tax and interest effects.

EBIT Margin = EBIT ÷ Revenue
= 344 ÷ 6410 = 5.37%
PAT Margin = 242 ÷ 6410 = 4%
Gross margin starts at 25% → EBIT margin falls to 5.4% → Net margin 4%. The gap between gross and net margin is mostly eaten by employee costs and other operating expenses (₹1,173 Cr). This is normal for capital-light operations with high marketing/admin costs.

Return on Equity (ROE)

The ultimate measure of shareholder value creation. If you invest ₹100 of equity capital, ROE tells you how much net profit the company earns on that ₹100.

ROE = PAT ÷ Equity (Shareholders' Fund)
= 242 ÷ 1409 = ~17%
₹17 return per ₹100 invested by shareholders

Cash Flow

Do profits actually become cash?

Cash is King, Profit is Queen

A company can show large profits on paper while running out of cash. This happens when sales are on credit (shows profit) but collection is slow (no cash). Cash flow ratios verify that profits are real.

Three cash flow activities:
🔵 CFO — Cash from Operations (core business)
🟠 CFI — Cash from Investing (capex, acquisitions)
🟣 CFF — Cash from Financing (debt, dividends)

CFO / EBITDA

What fraction of EBITDA actually becomes operating cash? Closer to 1 = better. A ratio of 1.0 would mean 100% of EBITDA is collected as cash.

CFO / EBITDA = Cash from Operations ÷ EBITDA
CFO = ₹323 Cr
EBITDA = ₹440 Cr
Ratio = 323 ÷ 440 = 0.73×
🟡 At 0.73× the ratio is on the lower side. As the company grows and collects its receivables more efficiently, this should move toward 1. Acceptable for now given high DSO.
CFO / EBITDA quality scale0.73×
0 (no cash)0.5 (weak)0.7 (ok)0.9 (good)1.0 (ideal)

CFO / Net Profit

Is the reported net profit backed by actual cash? Should ideally be ≥ 1.0.

CFO / Net Profit = Cash from Operations ÷ PAT
= 323 ÷ 242 = 1.25×
CFO / Net Profit = 1.25× — cash exceeds reported profits
🟢 Excellent! The company is generating ₹1.25 in cash for every ₹1 of reported net profit. This means profits are not just accounting numbers — they are real cash. This is a high-quality earnings signal.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

After running the business and reinvesting (capex), how much cash is left over? This is the cash available for dividends, buybacks, or further growth.

Free Cash Flow = CFO − Capital Expenditure (CFI)
A company that generates strong, growing FCF year after year is a machine. FCF growth = shareholder wealth creation. Always look at FCF trends alongside net profit trends.
High Free Cash Flow
FCF > 0
Company can self-fund growth, pay dividends, reduce debt
Negative FCF
FCF < 0
May be investing heavily (OK if growing) or burning cash (bad)

The Analyst's Checklist

Step 1 — Read Auditor's Report
Any red flags? Stop and move on. Clear? Continue.
Step 2 — Identify Major Items
Find top 3 assets by value. What are they? Understand why.
Step 3 — Check Solvency
D/E + ICR. Is the debt load manageable?
Step 4 — Check Liquidity
Current & quick ratio. Can it survive short-term?
Step 5 — Efficiency
Turnover ratios + DSO + CCC. Is capital deployed well?
Step 6 — Profitability
Margin trends + ROE + DuPont. Is value being created?
Step 7 — Cash Quality
CFO/EBITDA + CFO/PAT + FCF. Are profits real cash?
Step 8 — Track Trends
Compare 5 years. Improving trends > single year snapshots.
