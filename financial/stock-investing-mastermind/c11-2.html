7.3 Common Sizing

Everything as a
Percentage of Revenue

Raw numbers lie. ₹50 Cr net profit looks great — until you realise revenue was ₹5,000 Cr. Common sizing neutralises scale, letting you compare a company to itself across years and against peers.

Core Idea: Revenue = 100%. Every other line item is expressed as a % of revenue. When COGS shrinks as a percentage, gross profit grows — regardless of whether absolute numbers moved up or down.
Income Statement — Common Size View Revenue = 100%
What to watch: As COGS% declines over years, OPM% and Net Profit% rise. That's margin expansion — the hallmark of a company gaining pricing power or operational efficiency.

Balance Sheet — Common Size

Here, every asset is expressed as % of total assets, and every liability as % of total liabilities + equity.

Assets Side (% of Total Assets)
Liabilities Side (% of Total Liabilities + Equity)
Insight from the book: If receivables% is shrinking but inventory% is rising, the company is accumulating unsold goods — a warning sign even if top-line revenue is growing.

7.4 Key Performance Indicators

Beyond Sales & Profits —
What Actually Drives a Business

Every industry has non-financial metrics that reveal operational health before profits show up in reports. Identifying and tracking the right KPIs separates sharp investors from noise-followers.

The Rule: We must first choose 3–4 factors we believe are crucial to the operation of the business. These KPIs should be tracked every quarter.

7.5 Ownership Structure

Who Owns the Stock
Matters as Much as the Stock

The ownership composition of a company reveals confidence levels, alignment of interests, and where "smart money" is flowing. It's a living signal that changes every quarter.

👔
Promoters
Original founders. Own a sizable portion. Still running the company is a strong indicator of alignment.
High holding = 🟢 Bullish
🏢
Institutions
Mutual funds, insurance, offshore funds. Deep research teams. Called "smart money" for good reason.
Rising = 🟢 Confidence
🌟
Significant Investors
Famous, seasoned investors. Enormous followership. Their entry signals thorough due diligence.
Entry = Signal to study
👥
Public
Retail investors with limited research time. High public ownership = weaker corporate governance pressure.
High = Less accountability

Green & Red Signals in Ownership

📈
Promoter buying more
Promoters increasing stake = high conviction in company's future. Strongest internal signal.
🏦
Institutional holdings rising
More smart money accumulating = analysts have validated the business thesis.
⚠️
Rapid promoter selling
Small or gradual changes are fine. A sudden sharp drop in promoter stake in a short period is a red flag.
📉
Institutions consistently exiting
If smart money is leaving quarter after quarter, revisit your investment thesis immediately.
Track it like KPIs: Maintain a quarterly Excel sheet for shareholding patterns. When KPIs deteriorate AND ownership weakens simultaneously — that's the exit signal before it shows in financials.
