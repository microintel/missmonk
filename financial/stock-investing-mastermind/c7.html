Where to Find

Before you invest a single rupee, you need the right information. Chapter 2 maps out exactly where to look — and what to skip.

Core idea: Information about a company flows from two directions — directly from the company itself (primary) and from third-party analysts (secondary). Knowing which to trust, and how to use each, is the foundation of smart investing.

2.1 — Primary Sources

Straight from the
Horse's Mouth

These documents come directly from the company or its management — making them the most authoritative first-hand data you can get.

📋

Annual Report

Published every year (50–250 pages), it covers the company's full financials, KMPs (Key Managerial Personnel), employee details, and business commentary. Think of it as a company's complete health report card.

Real example Open Reliance Industries Annual Report → go to "Management Discussion & Analysis" → you'll find revenue breakdown by segment, capex plans, and risk factors all in one place.
Must Read
📊

Investor Presentation

Management creates 20–40 slide decks that give a structured, visual breakdown of the business. Far easier to skim than a full annual report. Published quarterly after results.

Real example A logistics company's investor deck will tell you: total fleet size (trucks/ships), quarterly revenue, industry growth rate, and market share — all in a few slides.
Must Read
🎙️

Conference Call (Con Call)

Quarterly calls where management addresses investors directly. Starts with a brief update, then Q&A. Transcripts are freely available on BSE/NSE or the company website. You hear management's tone and evasiveness — invaluable signals.

Real example In an Infosys Q2 con call, an analyst asked about visa cost headwinds. The CFO's measured answer vs. a defensive answer signals management quality.
Encouraged
🎤

Management Interviews

When the CEO/CFO appears on YouTube channels, CNBC, or podcasts. These are quick to consume (20–30 min) and give you a feel for how management thinks about competition, growth, and risk.

Real example Watching Zepto's founder on a startup podcast reveals their unit economics mindset far more directly than any press release.
Encouraged
📜

DRHP — Draft Red Herring Prospectus

Every company filing for an IPO must submit a DRHP to SEBI. It's 400–500 pages of exhaustive detail — business model, 5-year financials, risk factors, fund utilisation, promoter history. The most thorough document ever submitted by a company. As a retail investor, reading ~150 pages is enough.

Real example ONGC's DRHP (Sept 2011) listed its oil reserves, refinancing risks, and government stake details — information unavailable anywhere else in such structured form. Find DRHPs at sebi.gov.in.
Must Read
📢

Corporate Announcements

Companies file regular updates to BSE/NSE for any material event — new market entry, acquisition, key management change, board meeting outcome. These are mandatory disclosures that you can access free on bseindia.com or nseindia.com.

Real example Reliance announced its entry into the Jio retail energy market via a BSE corporate announcement months before it made mainstream news.
Monitor
2.2 — Secondary Sources

Third-Party Research
That's Worth Trusting

These come from organizations with no stake in any particular stock — making them relatively unbiased and useful for industry-level understanding.

Key principle: Consulting firms like Deloitte, Edelweiss, Arthur D Little, Knight Frank, and PwC have no incentive to favour any individual stock. Use their data for facts, not their opinions for buy/sell decisions.
🏭

Industry Reports

Consulting firms publish deep-dive reports on specific sectors — current trends, market size, CAGR projections, key risks, and regulatory outlook. Essential for understanding the playing field before you evaluate a specific company.

Real example A McKinsey or KPMG "Future of Indian Logistics" report tells you whether the entire sector is growing before you decide if a logistics company stock is worth investigating.
Encouraged
🏛️

Government Reports

Ministries like Ministry of Road Transport & Highways, Ministry of Power, or DPIIT publish annual data with actual numbers — number of registered vehicles, installed power capacity, FDI figures. Hard data with official credibility.

Real example The MoRTH Annual Report gives you exact data on National Highway construction km/year — crucial context if you're evaluating a roads infra company.
Skippable
🤝

Industry Associations

Every sector has a body — SIAM (automobiles), NASSCOM (IT), CII (general industry). They publish on-ground reports with member data, lobbying positions, and sector health. Not biased toward any single company.

Real example NASSCOM's annual IT industry report gives staffing trends, export revenue, and emerging tech adoption rates across the entire Indian IT sector.
Skippable
🏦

Brokerage Reports

Published by brokers like Motilal Oswal, Kotak, ICICI Securities. Contain deep financial models, segment breakdowns, and management commentary summaries. Ignore the Buy/Sell recommendation — use only the factual data they've aggregated.

Real example A Motilal Oswal report on Titan may contain 5-year sales data across segments (watches, jewellery, eyewear) that would take you hours to compile yourself. Take the data, discard the "BUY at ₹3200 target."
Skippable
2.3 — What to Avoid

Sources That
Mislead Investors

These can help spark investment ideas — but never use them to validate or make a buy/sell decision.

📰

Newspapers

Excellent for discovering new companies or sectors. Terrible for valuing them. News cycles create noise — stocks react to headlines, not fundamentals.

✓ OK for idea generation
📱

Media Articles

Often opinionated, driven by traffic incentives, and frequently influenced by PR. A "Top 5 Stocks for 2025" article has zero accountability if those picks lose money.

✓ OK as a starting point
✍️

Blogs & Social Posts

Anyone can write a blog or tweet about a stock. There's no verification, no accountability, and strong potential for pump-and-dump manipulation. Credibility is zero without track record.

✗ Never rely on these
The golden rule: Use media and blogs to discover ideas. Then verify everything independently through primary and secondary sources before making any decision. "I read it on Moneycontrol" is not a thesis.

🔄 The Right Research Flow

Step 1 — Idea Discovery (use media)

Read newspapers, watch YouTube, browse Screener.in screeners. Find a company that interests you.

Step 2 — Primary Research (use primary sources)

Read the latest Annual Report + Investor Presentation. Scan the DRHP if it's a newer company.

Step 3 — Context Check (use secondary sources)

Read an industry report. Skim 4 recent con-call transcripts. Grab factual data from a brokerage report.

Step 4 — Decision

Now — and only now — form your own investment thesis. Never outsource this step to media or blogs.

2.4 — Research Plan

How to Research in
4–5 Hours a Week

A retail investor can't dedicate 40+ hours like an institutional fund manager. Here's a prioritised system that works.

Important: If you genuinely cannot dedicate 4–5 hours per week to research, direct stock investing is not for you. In that case, mutual funds or a PMS (Portfolio Management Service) will give you better risk-adjusted outcomes.

🔴 Compulsory to Go Through

Never skip these
📋

Annual Report

Complete picture of financials and operations for the prior year. Essential to read every single year for every holding.

📊

Investor Presentation

20–30 minute read. Quarterly data on operations and financials. Faster than the full annual report.

📜

DRHP

One-time deep read (~150 pages for retail). The most comprehensive company document ever filed. Do this before any new position.

🔵 Encouraged to Go Through

High value when time allows
🏭

Industry Reports

Allocate time proportional to your portfolio exposure. 25% in textiles? Spend 25% of your industry research time there.

🎙️

Conference Calls

Read at least 4 con calls per company you analyse. Consecutive quarters reveal management consistency and tone shifts.

🎤

Management Interviews

Quick, high-signal. Watch/read as many as you can find. Reveals thinking quality of the people running your money.

⚪ You Can Skip

For institutional investors, not retail
🏦

Brokerage Reports

Time-consuming and buy/sell recommendations are unreliable. Only grab specific factual data if you need it.

🤝

Industry Associations

Useful but takes significant time to find and parse the right data. Lower ROI for retail investors.

🏛️

Government Reports

Dense, often delayed. Unless you're in a highly regulated sector like power or roads infra, you can skip these.

📐 Time Allocation = Portfolio Allocation

If 25% of your portfolio is in Textiles, spend ~25% of your industry research hours on textile reports.
