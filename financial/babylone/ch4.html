A Babylon Story — Chapter Four

Meet the Goddess of Good Luck

Back at the Temple of Learning, Arjun Rao opens the floor to a new question: is there actually a way to attract good luck — and were they all looking for her in the wrong places?

The Question for Tonight

Everyone wants to be lucky. Is there a way to actually attract it?

At the Temple of Learning, anyone could speak — a labourer could challenge a nobleman's opinion and be heard on equal footing. Most evenings, a crowd gathered in Arjun Rao's hall to debate whatever question someone raised. Tonight, a cloth weaver has one.

A cloth weaver

"This day I have been lucky — I found a purse with gold pieces in it. I'd very much like to keep being lucky. Since I suspect everyone here shares that wish, why don't we debate how good luck might actually be attracted?"

Arjun Rao

"A subject well worth our evening. To some, luck is pure chance. To others, it's a goddess who rewards those who please her. Let's find out — is there a way to invite her favour?"

He starts by asking if anyone else in the room has simply stumbled onto treasure the way the weaver did. He waits. Nobody answers.

"What, no one? Then rare indeed must be this kind of good luck."— Arjun Rao
First Guess: The Gaming Tables

Where men actually go looking for luck

A young man in the crowd suggests the obvious place — the gaming tables, where everyone hopes the goddess will smile on their bet. Someone in the back calls out, only half-joking, asking whether she actually did.

The young man, laughing

"I'll admit — she didn't seem to know I was there at all."

Arjun does the actual math with the room. In the popular cube game, one face out of six pays four times your bet; the other five faces lose it entirely.

"For each cast we have five chances to lose, but because the red pays four for one, we have four chances to win. In a night's play the game master can expect to keep for his profit a fifth of all the coins wagered."— Arjun Rao

Roll it enough times and the pattern always wins out — the house keeps a steady share of everything wagered, no matter how the room feels about any single cast. Arjun then asks the group a pointed question:

"Among my acquaintances are many of the successful men of Babylon, yet among them I am unable to name a single one who started his success from such a source."— Arjun Rao

Nobody in the room can name a single self-made success who started at the gaming tables either — not even themselves, once Arjun asks directly. Laughter and groans confirm it. Luck, it seems, isn't hiding there.

A Different Angle

An elderly merchant reframes the whole question

If a profitable trade is really just a reward for effort, and not luck — an elderly merchant suggests — then maybe they're looking in the wrong direction. What about the good luck that was right there, and simply wasn't taken?

Was it luck, or was it earned? Decide for each one.

The Merchant's Own Story

The land, the waterwheels, and the new robes

The merchant offers to go first. Years ago, as a newly married young man, his father urged him toward a real opportunity: a friend's son was raising money to buy dry land near the city, build ox-driven waterwheels to irrigate it, then divide and sell it as garden plots. Twelve investors were needed, each contributing one-tenth of their earnings until the land was ready.

His father

"My son, I want you to begin building your own estate now — and to learn from the mistakes I never corrected. Set aside one-tenth of your earnings for worthy investments like this one. Do it now, and by my age, you'll have built real wealth."

The young merchant

"I am young, Father. There's plenty of time."

His father

"I said the very same thing at your age. Opportunity waits for no man. Today it is here; soon it is gone. I beg you — do not delay."

But new robes had just arrived from the east — beautiful, expensive, exactly what he and his wife wanted. Committing a tenth of his earnings meant giving that up. He hesitated. He kept hesitating. By the time he considered it seriously, the opportunity had closed to new investors.

"The enterprise did prove to be more profitable than any man had prophesied. This is my tale, showing how I did permit good luck to escape."— The merchant
The Real Lesson

Good luck isn't a windfall. It's the first step, taken on time.

A weathered herdsman offers the sharpest reply. He didn't wait for the perfect opportunity either — he simply started, small, the moment he could.

H

A desert herdsman

Now owns many herds

"As a boy, I bought a single young calf with one piece of silver. That was the beginning of everything I own today. To take that first step — from a man who only earns by his own labour, to a man whose money earns for him — that is the best luck any man can have."

He points out something quietly devastating about the merchant's story: had he taken that step in his youth, he'd be a genuinely wealthy man today. The opportunity itself wasn't rare. Taking it, on time, was.

The Room Names the Real Culprit

There's a word for what actually cost him his luck

A visiting Syrian, still finding his footing in the language, tries to make his point anyway.

The Syrian's Question

"I wish to call this friend, the merchant, a name — not impolite, but I do not know your word for it. The man who puts off doing what is good for him. He waits. He says, 'I have much business right now — by and by I will talk to you.' What is this man called?"

"Procrastinator," someone calls out.

"That's him! Opportunity, she does not wait for such a slow fellow."

The merchant, good-natured about it, bows to the room in agreement.

One More Tale

Locked out of the city, at midnight, with nine hundred sheep

An animal trader offers the last story of the night. Returning from a failed ten-day search for camels, he finds the city gates already shut for the night. In the dark, a farmer approaches — his wife gravely ill, desperate to sell his entire flock and rush home.

The farmer, in the dark

"Please, honourable sir — pay me but two-thirds of the price tonight, so I may leave at once. My most trusted slave will stay to help count the flock properly at daylight, and you can pay him the balance then."

It was too dark to count nine hundred restless sheep. The trader refused to pay until he could verify the number himself in daylight — a reasonable-sounding condition.

What would you have done?

Before You Go

What this chapter is really saying

  • Luck doesn't live at the gaming tables — the math is quietly designed to favour whoever runs the game, every single time.
  • A profit earned through steady work isn't luck at all. It's a fair reward, and Arjun is careful to draw that line.
  • Real good luck looks like a genuine opportunity, met with quick action — not a windfall that falls in your lap.
  • The size of the first step barely matters. Taking it before you talk yourself out of it is what matters.
  • Hesitation has a name, and it has a cost — opportunity rarely waits around for anyone to feel fully ready.
