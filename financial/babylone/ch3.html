A Babylon Story — Chapter Three

Seven Cures for a Lean Purse

The king of Babylon asks his richest citizen a simple question: can wealth actually be taught? Arjun Rao says yes — and spends seven days teaching a hundred students exactly how.

The King's Problem

The city built great things — and somehow got poorer

After years of building huge public projects — canals, temples — the king returns victorious from war to find his city struggling. Work has dried up. Merchants have no customers. People can't afford food, despite all the money that was spent.

The Royal Chancellor

"The gold has found its way into the hands of a few very rich men. It filtered through the fingers of most of our people as quickly as goat's milk goes through a strainer. Now that the flow has stopped, most have nothing to show for their earnings."

The King

"Why should so few men be able to acquire all the gold?"

The Chancellor

"Because they know how, your majesty. One cannot fault a man for succeeding at something he understands."

The king asks a better question: if a few people know the secret, why not teach it to everyone? He summons the richest man in the city — Arjun Rao — and asks him directly.

The King

"Arjun, is there truly a secret to acquiring wealth? Can it be taught?"

Arjun Rao

"It is entirely practical, your majesty. What one man knows, he can teach to another."

Arjun agrees to teach a hundred chosen citizens — including, this time, Vignesh and Mahadevaswamy — the seven remedies that once fattened his own famously empty purse. Each day, for seven days, one cure.

Cure No. 1

Start thy purse to fattening

Arjun asks his students what work they do — a record-keeper, a butcher, an egg seller. Every trade, he says, is a stream of coins. The first cure is simply learning to keep a small part of that stream for yourself.

Arjun, to the egg merchant in the class

"If you place ten eggs in a basket each morning, and take out only nine each evening, what happens?"

The egg merchant

"In time... it would overflow."

"For every ten coins thou placest within thy purse take out for use but nine. Thy purse will start to fatten at once, and its increasing weight will feel good in thy hand."— Arjun Rao (Arkad, in the original story)

"Truth is always simple."— Arjun Rao

He adds a strange truth he can't fully explain: once he stopped spending more than nine-tenths of what he earned, he never actually felt shorter of money — and, oddly, coins seemed to come to him more easily than before.

Cure No. 2

Control thy expenditures

A student objects: what if my earnings barely cover my expenses already? Arjun has an answer ready.

"What each of us calls our 'necessary expenses' will always grow to equal our incomes, unless we protest to the contrary."— Arjun Rao
"Just as weeds grow in a field wherever the farmer leaves space for their roots, so freely do desires grow in men wherever there is a possibility of their being gratified."— Arjun Rao

Even Arjun, with all his wealth, cannot satisfy every desire — there isn't enough time, strength, or appetite in one lifetime. The fix isn't earning more. It's writing down every expense, separating what's truly necessary from what's simply wanted, and never touching the one-tenth that's set aside.

A student in a red and gold robe

"I am a free man. Isn't a budget just a kind of slavery — deciding in advance what I may and may not spend?"

Arjun Rao

"Would a pack-donkey, budgeting his own load, choose to carry jewels and bars of gold? No — he'd carry hay and water for the trail. A budget isn't there to limit your joy. It's a bright light in a dark cave, showing you exactly where your purse is leaking."

Cure No. 3

Make thy gold multiply

Coins sitting in a purse feel good, Arjun says, but they earn nothing on their own. The real goal isn't a big pile of savings — it's a steady stream of income that keeps flowing in, whether you're working or not.

"A man's wealth is not in the coins he carries in his purse; it is the income he buildeth — the golden stream that continually floweth into his purse and keepeth it always bulging."— Arjun Rao

To prove how powerful this becomes over time, Arjun tells his class about a farmer who, the day his son was born, gave ten pieces of silver to a moneylender — to be left untouched, earning a rental of one-fourth its value every four years, compounding the whole time.

At 20 years the book records it reaching about 30½ pieces. By 50 years, about 167 — nearly seventeen times the original, without the farmer lifting a finger after the first gift.

Arjun's own first profitable investment worked the same way: lending capital to a shield maker named Aggar, and — crucially — reinvesting the rental he earned each time, instead of spending it. That's the real secret: not just saving, but letting both the savings and their earnings keep working.

Cure No. 4

Guard thy treasures from loss

Arjun turns serious. Growing money is one thing — keeping it safe is another, and it's the lesson he learned most painfully.

"Misfortune loves a shining mark."— Arjun Rao

He reminds the class of his own first investment — the year his savings went to a friend chasing rare gems overseas, a friend who knew nothing about jewels. The gems were fake. The savings were gone.

"Be not too confident of thine own wisdom in entrusting thy treasures to the possible pitfalls of investments. Better by far to consult the wisdom of those experienced in handling money for profit."— Arjun Rao

The rule going forward is simple: protect the original amount before chasing a bigger return. Check whether the person can actually repay, and whether they're actually known for doing so. Ask someone who works with money professionally before trusting an exciting opportunity.

Cure No. 5

Make of thy dwelling a profitable investment

Too many families, Arjun says, spend their whole lives paying rent to a landlord — with a wife who has nowhere to grow a garden, and children who play in unclean lanes because there's no yard of their own.

"To a man's heart it brings gladness to eat the figs from his own trees and the grapes of his own vines. To own his own domicile... putteth confidence in his heart and greater effort behind all his endeavours."— Arjun Rao

His advice isn't "save up the full price of a house." It's the opposite: show a lender a reasonable portion you've already saved, borrow the rest, and redirect the same money you were already paying in rent toward paying that loan off instead. Every payment builds ownership rather than disappearing into a landlord's pocket.

Cure No. 6

Insure a future income

Every life moves the same direction — childhood toward old age. Arjun's sixth lesson is blunt: plan now for the years you'll no longer be able to earn, and for a family that may need to stand on its own without you.

"A lean purse to a man no longer able to earn, or to a family without its head, is a sore tragedy."— Arjun Rao

Arjun goes further, describing an idea he can only imagine — a plan where many men each pay in a small, regular sum, and the combined pool provides a real fortune for any one family should its earner pass away:

"Some day wise-thinking men will devise a plan to insure against death whereby many men pay in but a trifling sum regularly, the aggregate making a handsome sum for the family of each member who passeth to the beyond."— Arjun Rao

He's describing, almost exactly, what we now call life insurance — centuries before it existed.

Cure No. 7

Increase thy ability to earn

On the last day, Arjun doesn't talk about gold at all. He talks about the people sitting in front of him.

A young man, once asking Arjun for a loan

"My earnings simply aren't enough to cover my expenses. Six times in two months I've asked my master for a raise — without success. No man can ask more often than that."

Arjun Rao

"What you need, young man, isn't a loan. It's to earn more. What are you actually doing to increase your capacity to earn?"

"Preceding accomplishment must be desire. Thy desires must be strong and definite. General desires are but weak longings."— Arjun Rao

Wanting to "be rich" leads nowhere. Wanting five specific gold pieces, and working out exactly how to get them, does — because succeeding at a small definite goal trains you to succeed at a bigger one next.

Climb the desire ladder

Tap the bottom rung first — each one unlocks the next, just as Arjun describes.

He also lists what he considers non-negotiable, for any man who respects himself:

  • Pay his debts with all the promptness within his power
  • Take care of his family, so they think and speak well of him
  • Make a will, so his property is honourably divided if the Gods call him
  • Have compassion for those struck by misfortune, and help within reason
"There is more gold in Babylon, my students, than thou dreamest of. There is abundance for all."— Arjun Rao, closing his seventh lesson
All Seven, Together

What Arjun actually taught his hundred students

  • Cure 1 — Start thy purse to fattening: keep at least one coin in ten for yourself, before spending on anything.
  • Cure 2 — Control thy expenditures: a budget doesn't limit your freedom, it shows you exactly where your money is leaking.
  • Cure 3 — Make thy gold multiply: real wealth is an income stream, built by letting savings and their earnings both stay invested.
  • Cure 4 — Guard thy treasures from loss: protect the original amount first, and get advice only from people who genuinely understand money.
  • Cure 5 — Own thy own home: money spent on rent forever is gone forever; money spent paying off your own roof eventually stops costing anything.
  • Cure 6 — Insure a future income: a small, regular habit, left untouched for years, quietly becomes a real safety net.
  • Cure 7 — Increase thy ability to earn: chase specific, small goals first — the skill of achieving them is what makes bigger ones possible.
