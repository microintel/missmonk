Why two hardworking friends — one who fixes engines, one who plays music — never seemed to have money left over, no matter how much they earned.

Meet Vignesh

He built the best bikes in town. His wallet still stayed empty.

In an old book called The Richest Man in Babylon, a chariot-builder named Bansir sits on a wall outside his shop, too discouraged to work. Let's meet him again, closer to home.

V

Vignesh

Bike mechanic, runs a small garage in his town

Vignesh has fixed bikes for fifteen years. Everyone says he's the best mechanic around. Customers line up outside his shop. Yet on payday, after rent, groceries, and his son's school fees, there is almost nothing left. He is good at his work. He is not good at keeping any of the money it brings.

One evening, Vignesh is sitting outside his garage, staring at a half-repaired bike, when his old friend Mahadevaswamy walks up, guitar case in hand.

M

Mahadevaswamy

Plays at weddings and events

Mahadevaswamy is a well-loved wedding singer. He earns good money on a big wedding season — and spends most of it before the next one arrives.

"If I did have two shekels, to no one could I lend them — for they would be my entire fortune. No one lends his entire fortune, not even to his best friend."— Bansir, in the original story

Mahadevaswamy jokes, asking to borrow a little money. Vignesh admits he has almost none — despite years of steady, skilled work. Both men realize something at the same time: they have been earning for years, but neither of them has ever actually become richer.

The Real Lesson

A full wallet is not the same as being rich

The book makes one sharp point: a wallet full of cash empties fast if nothing refills it. What actually matters is having a steady stream of money flowing in — an income — not just a one-time pile of savings. Try both below.

Why Hard Work Alone Wasn't Enough

More years of work didn't mean more wealth

Vignesh has worked harder every year — more bikes, more customers, longer hours. But his savings never grew, because everything he earned went straight back out. Compare that to what happens once someone actually learns how money can grow.

Vignesh — years of hard work, spends it all Someone who learns the secret of keeping some, and growing it

This is the moment both friends realize: they have never once asked how money grows. They have only ever worked, earned, and spent. So they decide to do something neither of them has tried before — go ask someone who already knows.

The Turning Point

They decide to ask someone who already has what they want

Vignesh remembers an old school friend, Arjun Rao, who started with nothing and is now one of the wealthiest people in town — the kind of person banks call for advice. Mahadevaswamy had seen him recently, riding past in a new car, and Arjun had waved warmly, not looked away.

Mahadevaswamy

"They say his own son went off to another city and became rich too, without any help from his father. Arjun must know something we don't."

Vignesh

"It costs nothing to ask a good friend for advice. We've spent our whole lives getting better at our trade. We never once spent an hour learning how money actually works. Let's go ask him."

That single decision — to stop guessing and go learn from someone who has already succeeded — is the real beginning of the story. Not a new skill, not a lucky break. Just the willingness to ask.

What this chapter is really saying

  • Working hard and earning well does not automatically make someone wealthy — what happens to the money afterward matters just as much.
  • A wallet full of cash is not the same as an income. One runs out. The other keeps refilling.
  • Most people spend a lifetime getting better at their craft, but never spend any time learning how money itself works.
  • The fastest way to learn is often the simplest: find someone who has already achieved what you want, and ask them how.
