The Question

"We studied together. We played together. Why are you rich and we are not?"

Arjun Rao is generous — with his family, his charities, his own comforts. Yet every year, he somehow ends up wealthier than the year before, no matter how much he spends. Old classmates like Vignesh can't understand it. They ask him straight out.

An old classmate

"We studied under the same teacher. We were never behind you in our lessons. In the years since, you haven't worked harder than us, or been more honest than us. Why has luck picked you, and not us?"

Arjun Rao

"If you have only managed a bare living since we were students, it's not because luck skipped you. It's because you never learned the rules that build wealth — or you know them, and don't follow them."

Arjun explains something most people never consider: money that shows up by luck — a lottery win, a surprise inheritance — usually disappears fast. People either spend it all in a blur, or clutch it so tightly out of fear that they never actually enjoy it. Very few people know how to take money, earned or unearned, and make it grow. Arjun is one of them. And he learned it the hard way.

Where It Started

A clerk with no savings, and one impossible deadline

Arjun grew up the son of a small shopkeeper — no land, no inheritance coming his way. He took a job as a record-keeper, copying documents by hand for a wage that vanished into rent and food each month. Nothing left over, same as everyone else.

S

Shankar Lal

An old moneylender, known as the richest client in the records office

One day Shankar Lal walked in needing an urgent copy of a long contract — due in two days, for a payment of two coins. Arjun worked hard, but the document was too long. He missed the deadline.

Arjun, to Shankar Lal (missing the deadline)

"You are a wealthy man. Tell me the secret to becoming rich, and I will work through the night — by sunrise, this will be finished."

Shankar Lal

"You're a bold one. Very well — it's a deal."

Arjun worked through the night, eyes burning from the lamp smoke, and finished by sunrise. Shankar Lal kept his word.

"I found the road to wealth when I decided that a part of all I earned was mine to keep. And so will you."— Shankar Lal (Algamish, in the original story)

Arjun was confused — wasn't everything he earned already his to keep? Shankar Lal laughed. Rent, food, clothes, little expenses here and there — by the end of the month, everything was gone. Arjun was paying everyone — the landlord, the tailor, the grocer — except himself. Shankar Lal's advice was simple: set aside at least one-tenth of everything earned, before spending on anything else. Pay yourself first.

It Didn't Work The First Time

Arjun tried this for three straight years — and got it wrong twice

Saving one-tenth of his wage turned out to be easy. Arjun barely noticed the difference. But knowing how to save is not the same as knowing what to do with savings. Tap through what actually happened, year by year.

01

He trusted a friend who wasn't an expert

Arjun handed his year of savings to a friend who claimed he could import rare gems and resell them at a huge profit. The friend was a brickmaker — not a jeweller. The "gems" turned out to be coloured glass.

₹0 left
Lesson: never take advice about money from someone who isn't actually skilled in that area — ask the jeweller about gems, not the brickmaker.
02

He earned a return — and spent all of it

Undeterred, Arjun saved again and lent it to a metal trader, earning a steady payout every few months. But each time the payout arrived, he spent it — on feasts, on a new tunic, on small luxuries.

Savings frozen, earnings spent
Lesson: spending the returns is like eating your own children — the savings never multiply, because nothing is ever left to keep earning.
03

He finally let it compound

This time, Arjun let both his savings and their earnings stay invested. His money began earning money, and that money began earning more. Two years later, Shankar Lal returned — impressed enough to make Arjun his business partner.

Growing on its own
Lesson: your savings are workers. Let their wages also go to work. That's how a small seed becomes a tree.

On Willpower

It isn't willpower. It's just not skipping the small thing.

Years later, a friend calls Arjun lucky, and another calls him strong-willed. Arjun disagrees with both.

Arjun Rao

"Willpower is nothing grand. It's just the plain refusal to leave a task you set for yourself unfinished. If I told myself, 'for a hundred days, I'll pick up a pebble on my walk and drop it in the river' — I would do exactly that. Not skip a day and tell myself I'd throw in two tomorrow. Just the one pebble, every day, without fail."

Try it below — the same small, boring habit, repeated daily, is what actually built Arjun's fortune. Not one dramatic decision, but one un-skipped small one, repeated.

What Arjun Tells His Friends

Six things he says to anyone who asks

Tap each one to read what Arjun actually says.

1Pay yourself first▾
Set aside at least one-tenth of everything you earn, before any other spending. Arrange the rest of your expenses around it, not the other way round. Say it to yourself until it becomes automatic: a part of all I earn is mine to keep.
2Make your savings work▾
Money sitting idle earns nothing. Put it to work so it earns more money — and let that new money also stay invested, so it earns even more. This is how a small seed becomes a tree.
3Guard it with expert advice▾
Ask people who actually work with money for a living, not a well-meaning friend who's never handled an investment. A safe, modest return beats a risky, exciting one.
4Plan for old age▾
A day comes when you can no longer earn. Set something up now — through small, regular contributions — so that future version of you is provided for.
5Protect your family▾
Make sure your family won't struggle if something happens to you. This, too, can be arranged through small regular payments — no need to wait until you have a large sum.
6Still enjoy your life▾
Don't strain yourself trying to save more than is comfortable. If one-tenth is right for you, that's enough. Live well on the rest — life is meant to be enjoyed, not just hoarded against.
Before You Go

What this chapter is really saying

  • Wealth wasn't luck for Arjun — it started the moment he decided a part of every rupee earned belonged to him first.
  • His first two attempts to grow that money actually failed. Failure wasn't the problem — quitting after failure would have been.
  • Money you spend as soon as it's earned never multiplies. Money left alone to compound eventually builds real wealth.
  • "Willpower" is really just not skipping the small, boring habit — one day at a time, without exception.
  • Anyone offering advice about your money should actually be experienced in that exact subject — not just willing to help.
