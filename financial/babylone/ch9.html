A story dug out of five-thousand-year-old clay

The Richest Man
in Babylon

In 1934, a professor translates five clay tablets found in the ruins of Babylon. They belong to a man named Dabasir — a camel trader who was once sold as a slave because of his debts. What he carved onto the clay is still one of the simplest money plans ever written.

Where the story begins

A letter from a dusty dig site

A British team is digging in the ruins of an old Mesopotamian city. Among the broken pottery, they find five clay tablets covered in strange writing. A professor in England spends weeks translating them.

He expects old myths and adventure stories. Instead, he finds something surprising: a man from five thousand years ago, writing down exactly how he paid off his debts and became wealthy. Nothing about money, it turns out, has really changed.

Tablet one

Who was Dabasir?

Dabasir was a camel trader in Babylon who lived beyond his means. He borrowed from friends, from a jeweller, from his landlord, from a moneylender — ten people in total. When the debts piled up, instead of facing them, he ran away to another city looking for quick riches.

It went badly. He ended up broke, then kidnapped, then sold as a slave in Syria. Years later, he escaped and made his way back home to Babylon — with nothing, but with a plan given to him by an old friend, a moneylender named Mathon.

TABLET I

Mathon's Rule

"That man who keepeth in his purse both gold and silver that he need not spend is good to his family and loyal to his king. But the man who hath naught in his purse is unkind to his family, for his own heart is bitter."

In plain words: if you never keep anything for yourself, money will always make you anxious and bitter — no matter how much you earn.

Tablet two

The plan: split every payday into three

Dabasir owed 119 pieces of silver and 141 pieces of copper to ten different people. Instead of panicking, Mathon gave him one simple rule to follow every single time he earned money.

1/10
Keep it. Set aside one-tenth of everything earned, and never touch it. This becomes real savings — money that works for you later.
7/10
Live on it. Seven-tenths covers food, rent, clothes, and a little enjoyment. The rule: never spend more than this share, no matter what.
2/10
Pay it back. Two-tenths goes to every creditor, split fairly between all of them, every single payday, without skipping.

Tablets three, four and five

Twelve months, one clay tablet at a time

Not every creditor was kind about it. Ahmar called him names. Alkahad, the house owner, threatened trouble. Dabasir didn't argue — he simply kept showing up with his small, honest payment, moon after moon.

Some months were good — he'd catch a fine herd of camels and earn 42 pieces of silver. Some months were lean — only 11 pieces. But the one-tenth was always saved, and the two-tenths always paid, no matter how small.

TABLET V

The last payment

After twelve full moons, Dabasir finished paying every single creditor. Old Alkahad — once his harshest critic — told him: "Thou wert once a piece of soft clay to be pressed by any hand. Now thou art bronze, capable of holding an edge."

The same plan, today

Three friends, one payday

The plan works the same today as it did in Babylon. Picture three friends, Vignesh, Pranav and Dhanush, each getting their monthly pay and choosing to follow Dabasir's rule.

V

Vignesh

Puts one-tenth of every paycheck into a separate savings account before he spends on anything else. He never sees that money as "available."

Pays himself first
P

Pranav

Still has an old laptop loan. He automates two-tenths of his salary to go straight to repayment the day he's paid — before he can spend it elsewhere.

Pays debts on schedule
D

Dhanush

Built his monthly budget around living on seven-tenths of his income — rent, food, travel, fun — and refuses to let it creep higher.

Lives on the rest

None of them earn a huge salary. What changes their situation is the order they do things in — save first, pay debts on purpose, then live on what's left.

Two years later

The professor tries it himself

The same professor who translated the tablets was quietly drowning in his own debts. He and his wife tried Dabasir's exact plan: twenty percent to creditors, ten percent kept, seventy percent to live on.

They asked every person they owed money to for patience, showed them the numbers, and stuck to the plan without exception. Within about two years, every debt was gone — and they had a growing investment fund besides.

The takeaway

What to do, and what to avoid

Strip away the camels and the clay, and the whole story comes down to a short list of habits.

Do this

  • Save a fixed portion the moment you're paid, before spending anything
  • Build your spending to fit inside what's left, on purpose
  • Pay every debt something, every time, even if it's small
  • Be honest with people you owe, and stay consistent
  • Keep going even through the bad months

Avoid this

  • Don't wait for "extra" money to start saving — it never arrives on its own
  • Don't run away from debts instead of facing them
  • Don't let spending quietly grow past what you earn
  • Don't pay one creditor in full while ignoring the rest
  • Don't give up the plan the first time a month goes badly
✦

One rule, five thousand years old

Save a tenth. Live on seven-tenths. Pay your debts with the rest. Dabasir carved it into clay so it would outlast him — and somehow, it has.
