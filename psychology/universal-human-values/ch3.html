A simple guide to human values

What do you really want in life?

Not the next exam. Not the next salary hike. Not the next thing to buy. There is something deeper we are all quietly chasing — and once you see it clearly, life gets a lot less confusing.

Where it all starts

A never-ending chain

Think about your own life for a second. You studied hard to get into a good school. Then you worked hard to get into a good college. Then you chased a good job. Then a good salary. Then a bigger house, a better car.

The moment you get one thing, your mind already jumps to the next thing. It never really stops and says, "okay, I'm done." So here is the real question — out of this whole chain, what is the one thing you're actually running towards?

The one honest answer

Everybody wants the same thing

Forget school, job, money for a second. Ask yourself these three simple questions honestly. Tap each one.

Do you want to be happy? +
Of course. Nobody wakes up wanting to feel bad. This is simply how we are made.
Do you want a good, comfortable life? +
Yes — enough money, a decent home, good health. Everyone wants this too. This is called "being prosperous".
Do you want this happiness to last forever, not just for a moment? +
Yes again. Nobody wants to be happy today and miserable tomorrow. We want it to stay.

Notice something? Every single person on earth answers "yes" to all three. Rich or poor, young or old — this doesn't change. This is called our basic aspiration: to be happy, to be well-off, and for it to last. Everything else — school, job, salary — is just a means to try and reach this one thing.

A quiet, honest check

So... are you there yet?

We all want to be happy, always. But if you ask yourself honestly right now — "am I happy? Is it lasting?" — most people pause. There's a gap between what we want and what we actually feel.

This gap is not naturally okay with us. So the real question becomes: what are we doing wrong, and what can actually close this gap?

A common trap

We chase things, hoping happiness follows

Most of us quietly believe: "once I have enough money, a good house, a good car — I'll finally be happy." So we work harder and harder for more and more things. But does it actually work?

P

Pranav software engineer, 28

Pranav got his first job and was thrilled with his first salary. A year later, he got a hike — happy for two days, then back to normal. Another hike, another two days of joy. He kept telling himself, "once I earn double, I'll finally relax." He now earns four times what he used to. He still says the same sentence. The money kept growing. The peace never showed up.

This isn't Pranav's fault — it's how most of us are taught to think. We assume things (money, gadgets, a bigger house) will automatically bring happiness. But notice: a cow with enough grass in its stomach is fully content, sitting and relaxed. A human being with enough food, enough clothes, enough of everything — usually isn't. We simply move on to worrying about the next thing. Things are necessary for us, but things alone are never enough.

"For an animal, food and shelter are enough. For a human being, they're necessary — but never quite enough on their own."
What actually fills the gap

Three things we truly need

If money and things alone can't do it, what else is needed? Turns out, a fulfilled life rests on three things — like a tree. Tap each part below.

1 · ROOTS — Right Understanding

This means understanding yourself clearly — what really makes you happy, and what your relationships and needs actually are. Just like roots you can't see, this sits quietly underneath everything else, and it's what everything else grows from.

These three matter in a priority order: understanding first, relationships second, things (money, comfort) third. Not because things don't matter — they do — but because understanding helps you know how much you truly need, and good relationships are what actually make daily life feel warm and worth living.

A small moment, a big lesson

The broken window

M

Mahadevaswamy's family a true-to-life moment

Mahadevaswamy's wife was cooking when she heard glass shatter — their two young sons were playing cricket indoors and had broken a window. Her old instinct was to rush out and scold them hard. But she paused and thought, "I can get this window fixed for a small amount. But if I lose my sons' trust and affection over this, that will cost so much more, and it can't be bought back." She walked out calmly and asked, "Are you boys okay, did the glass hurt you?" Her younger son, surprised, asked, "Aren't you going to shout at us?" — because that's what he was used to.

A broken window is a "thing" problem — small, fixable, cheap. A hurt relationship is much bigger, and money can't repair it. This little pause — thing first, or person first? — is a choice we make many times a day without realising it.

A helpful comparison

Animal mode vs. human mode

A cow needs grass. Once its stomach is full, it happily sits and rests — nothing more needed. That's fine for a cow — it's living exactly how it's supposed to.

But a human being trying to live only on "things" — more money, more gadgets, more comparison — never feels that same fullness. We were built to need more: real understanding of ourselves, and real warmth with other people. When we forget this and live only for things, we end up restless, competitive, and often unfair to the people around us — even without meaning to.

"It's fine for a cow to live on grass alone. It isn't fine for a person to live on money alone."
Bringing it together

So, what should you actually do?

Do this

  • Take a few honest minutes to understand what you truly want — not what ads or social media tell you to want.
  • Give real time to family and friends, not just leftover time after work.
  • Earn and use money wisely — it's needed, so plan for it sensibly.
  • When you have more than enough, help someone who has less.
  • Notice the small choices — thing vs. person — and let the person matter more.

Avoid this

  • Don't assume more money automatically means more happiness. It usually doesn't.
  • Don't keep postponing relationships for "later, once I'm settled."
  • Don't measure your worth only by salary, house size, or possessions.
  • Don't let a small "thing" problem turn into a big "relationship" problem.
  • Don't copy a lifestyle just because everyone around you is chasing it.
One last thought

Where do you want to end up?

Understanding yourself, caring for people, and using things wisely — in that order — is what actually leads to lasting happiness. It's simple to say, but it takes a daily choice to live it. The good news: you get to choose again, starting today.

"Understanding first. People second. Things third. Get this order right, and everything else tends to fall into place."
