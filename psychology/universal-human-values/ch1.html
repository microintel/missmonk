02 — What "value" really means

Value simply means: how you help the things around you

A pen is valuable because it can write. A plant is valuable because it feeds us. In the same way, a person's value is the good they naturally bring to the people and world around them — inside themselves, in their family, in society, and in nature. And here's the interesting part: doing this actually makes you feel happy too. It's not a sacrifice — it's a two-way gift.

03 — Two different questions

"What to do" comes before "how to do it"

In life we're always solving two kinds of questions. Most schools and colleges are very good at teaching one of them — but almost forget the other.

What to do? → Values

This is about deciding the right direction — what's actually worth doing with your time, money, and skill. This is the part that gives your life meaning.

How to do it? → Skills

This is your technical know-how — coding, medicine, management, machines. Skill makes you effective, but it doesn't tell you which way to point that ability.

Think of a train ticket. The clerk doesn't ask "how fast do you want to go" — first he asks "where do you want to go?" Skills are the speed of the train. Values decide the destination. Going very fast in the wrong direction doesn't help anyone.
04 — Seeing it in real people

Three familiar stories

These ideas are easier to see in people than in theory. Here are three simple, everyday examples.

P
Skill without direction

Pranav — chasing money, missing life

Pranav is brilliant at his job and earns very well. But he skips family dinners, ignores his health, and feels tired and empty even after a big salary hike. He has the "how" completely sorted — but never paused to ask "what am I actually doing this for?"

Lesson: Skill without a clear "why" can make you successful but not fulfilled.

V
Borrowed goals

Vignesh — living someone else's plan

Vignesh picked his college branch because his friends did. He's now choosing a career the same way, and feels quietly confused and directionless, even though he's "doing well" on paper. He hasn't yet asked himself what he actually wants.

Lesson: A goal that isn't your own rarely feels fulfilling, even when you achieve it.

M
Values + skill together

Mahadevaswamy — skill used with purpose

Mahadevaswamy, a small-town veterinary doctor, could easily charge high fees in a big city. Instead he treats farm animals in his own village at fair rates, because he knows what it means to a farmer's family when their only cow falls sick. His skill and his sense of what matters are pointed the same way.

Lesson: When skill is guided by a clear value, the same hard work becomes deeply fulfilling — for him and for the people he helps.

05 — Putting it into practice

So, what should you actually do?

You don't need to memorise any theory. Just keep these in mind as you go about ordinary life.

Do this

  • Pause sometimes and ask: "Is this really what I want, or am I just copying someone?"
  • Notice that you feel genuinely happy whenever you help family, friends, or strangers — that feeling is a clue, not a coincidence.
  • Learn skills, but also decide clearly what purpose they will serve.
  • Treat your health, your relationships, your work and nature as equally important — not one at the cost of the other.
  • Question your own beliefs sometimes instead of just repeating what you've always heard.

Avoid this

  • Don't chase money or status while quietly ignoring health and relationships — it rarely ends up feeling worth it.
  • Don't let skill run ahead of purpose — a powerful tool used carelessly can harm more than it helps.
  • Don't borrow your life goals from friends, trends, or social media without checking if they're really yours.
  • Don't treat nature as something separate from you — using it up carelessly comes back to affect your own life.
  • Don't assume a belief is true just because you've heard it repeated often — check it for yourself.
