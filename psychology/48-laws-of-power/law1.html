The 48 Laws of Power · Law 1

Never Outshine
the Master

There can be only one sun in the sky. If you shine brighter than the person above you, don't be surprised when they push you back into the dark.

Scroll to learn how ↓
In Simple Words

What does this law actually mean?

No fancy language. Just the idea, plain and clear.

Everyone above you — your boss, your teacher, your senior, your client — likes to feel important, smart, and in control. If you show off too much in front of them, you don't impress them. You make them feel small.

And a person who feels small doesn't stay grateful. They get insecure. And insecure people in power don't fix the problem by improving themselves — they fix it by removing the person who made them feel that way. That person might be you.

This doesn't mean "be less talented." It means: be smart about how you show your talent. Let your senior's light look bigger, even while you're the one actually glowing.

Proof From History

Broke the Law
Fouquet's Party

France, 1661. A finance minister throws the most spectacular party the king has ever seen — to win his favor.

Tap to see what happened →
Nicolas Fouquet was rich, clever, and loved lavish parties. To win back King Louis XIV's favor, he threw the grandest party in French history at his new château — top chefs, fireworks, a play written just for the king.

It backfired completely. Every gasp of admiration from the guests made the king feel smaller, not more loved. Louis saw his own finance minister outshining him in his own kingdom.

The very next day, Fouquet was arrested. He spent his last 20 years alone in a mountain prison.
Used the Law
Galileo's Trick

Italy, 1610. A scientist discovers the moons of Jupiter — and hands the glory to someone else.

Tap to see what happened →
Galileo discovered four moons circling Jupiter. Instead of keeping the credit for himself, he told the powerful Medici family that the moons "matched" their family of four brothers — as if the heavens themselves were celebrating the Medicis.

He made the discovery about their greatness, not his own genius.

Cosimo II instantly made Galileo his official court scientist, with a full salary for life.

The Takeaway

What to do — and what never to do

✅ Do
  • Make your boss or senior look good in front of others — especially in public or in front of clients.
  • Give credit to them, even for your own ideas. Let it look like teamwork, or their guidance.
  • Ask for their advice sometimes. People love feeling needed and useful.
  • Notice what makes them feel insecure — and quietly avoid poking that spot.
🚫 Don't
  • Correct or embarrass your senior in front of a group, even if you're right.
  • Grab credit that should go to your boss, even by accident.
  • Assume that being liked today means you're safe forever.
  • Show off just to prove you're the smartest person in the room.
One Exception

If your "master" is already weak, failing, or on their way out — you don't need to protect their ego forever. But be careful: attacking a weak boss too soon can make you look cruel. Read the room before you act.
