The 48 Laws of Power · Law 6

Court Attention
At All Cost

If people don't notice you, you have no power — no matter how good you are. This law is about one thing: making sure the spotlight finds you.

Scroll to begin

Plain and simple

What this law actually means

Being skilled at what you do is not enough. If nobody sees your work, it's as if it doesn't exist. The world notices loud, unusual, memorable people first — and asks questions about their talent later.

So this law says: build something about yourself that people can't ignore. A style. A story. A habit. Anything that makes your name travel from one mouth to the next.

Act I

Be impossible to ignore

The fastest way to get noticed is to do something people have never seen before — even if it sounds a little crazy.

True story · P.T. Barnum

In the 1800s, a showman named P.T. Barnum ran a museum in New York. To pull in crowds, he once paid a poor man to walk the streets carrying bricks — laying one down, picking another up, again and again, saying nothing to anyone. People stopped to stare. They followed him just to figure out what he was doing. By the end of the day, thousands had walked into his museum.

Barnum did this over and over. He spread a rumor that one of his performers was secretly a robot. He displayed a "mermaid" that was really a monkey stitched to a fish — and let the whole country argue about whether mermaids were real. None of it needed to be true. It only needed to be talked about.

"All we need to ensure success is notoriety." — the lesson Barnum learned early in his career

Vignesh and the glowing sculpture

At Vignesh's college fest, every project table looked the same — neat posters, quiet explanations. Vignesh built a strange glowing sculpture that half the students didn't even understand. People stopped, argued about what it meant, and took photos anyway. He didn't win "Best Project" on paper — but every recruiter at the fest remembered his name, not the winner's.

Act II

Or do the opposite — stay a little mysterious

Sometimes the loudest move is saying less. When people can't fully figure you out, they keep watching.

True story · Mata Hari

A woman named Margaretha Zelle arrived in Paris with almost no money and no dance training. Instead of blending in with the city's many performers, she invented a new identity: "Mata Hari," a dancer of mysterious Eastern origin. Every time a journalist asked about her past, she told a different story — India, Java, Sumatra, none of it true.

People couldn't pin her down, so they never stopped talking about her. Ambassadors and royalty competed for invitations to watch her perform. She wasn't the most beautiful or the most skilled dancer in Paris — but she was the most unexplained, and that made her the most famous.

People get tired of things they fully understand. What they can't fully explain, they keep watching.

Pranav's quiet confidence

Pranav never posts everything he's working on. When friends ask what he's building, he just smiles and says, "You'll see." No details, no proof, no hurry. People keep asking. He never needed to convince anyone — the not-knowing did the work for him.

Keys to power

Four small rules that make this work

01

Pick one clear trait

A style of dress, a way of speaking, a signature idea. Attach your name to it early, so people have something to remember you by.

02

Mystery can be small

You don't need a big secret. Just don't explain every detail about yourself. A little unfinished sentence is enough to keep people curious.

03

Refresh yourself often

The painter Picasso kept shocking his own fans with new styles. If you stay too predictable, people get bored and move to someone newer.

04

Nobody? Aim high

If you're unknown, a bold and public opinion about someone famous or powerful can get your own name noticed fast — but use this rarely.

The other side

Know when to step back

Chasing attention has one big trap: doing it at the wrong moment, in front of the wrong person.

True story · Lola Montez

Lola Montez was famous for commanding a room. But one evening at a London theatre, she made her entrance late — walking in dramatically, dropping her fur coat to reveal a bright red gown, moments after the Queen had already taken her royal seat. It looked like she was trying to outshine royalty, in public.

The whole room went cold. The royal couple pointedly looked away, and everyone followed their lead. From that night on, doors across London society closed to her.

Attention isn't power if it makes you look like you're competing with someone more powerful than you.

Dhanush at the farewell

At a senior colleague's farewell speech, Dhanush kept cracking jokes and pulling the room's focus onto himself all evening. Later, people quietly asked, "why does he always need to be the center of everything?" The attention he grabbed that night worked against him — it wasn't his moment to shine.

Before you try this

What to do, what to avoid

Do

  • Build one clear, memorable trait people can recognize you by.
  • Treat both praise and criticism as useful — neither one hurts your name.
  • Keep a little mystery. You don't have to explain everything about yourself.
  • Refresh your image now and then, before people get bored of it.

Don't

  • Don't play it so safe that you become invisible — that's the real failure.
  • Don't let "mystery" turn into people simply not trusting you.
  • Don't outshine someone more powerful than you, right in front of them.
  • Don't repeat the exact same trick forever — it stops working.
