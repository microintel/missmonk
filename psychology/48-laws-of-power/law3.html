Hide What You Really Want Nobody can block a plan they never saw coming

A short, simple guide to one of the oldest tricks in power: don't tell people what you're really after — let them find out when it's too late to stop you.

👆 tap to clear the smoke
If people know exactly what you want, they can plan against you. If they don't, they can't.
Start here

What's the big idea?

Most people say exactly what they think and want, all the time. It feels honest. But it also makes them easy to read — and easy to stop. This law says: keep your real goal quiet, and let people see something else instead.

From the book

Four old stories, told simply

Seduction

The love coach who taught patience

An older, wise woman named Ninon coached a young man who liked a countess but kept failing with her. Her advice: stop chasing. Act distant. Show up with other women so the countess gets jealous. Disappear, then reappear somewhere new. Keep her guessing.

It worked — the countess grew curious, then interested, then hooked. But one day the young man got impatient. He grabbed her hands and blurted out that he loved her. The moment he said it out loud, the magic broke. She felt tricked and pulled away for good.

Lesson: the moment people can clearly see what you're after, they get their guard back up. The game only works while your goal stays hidden.
Politics

The speech that fooled a whole country

A young politician named Bismarck actually wanted war with a rival country — but not yet, because he knew his side would lose right now. So he stood up and gave a passionate speech against war, even praising the enemy. Everyone was stunned. People assumed he had changed his mind.

His "peace-loving" speech impressed the king so much that Bismarck was promoted, again and again, until he was the most powerful man in government. Years later, once his side was strong enough, he led that very same war — and won it.

Lesson: saying the opposite of what you truly believe can throw everyone off balance long enough for you to gain real power.
Classic con

The boxer who "died"

A con man wanted to trick a rich businessman out of a large sum of cash. Instead of asking for money directly, he first pulled him into an exciting, totally different deal — buying land cheap and reselling it for a huge profit. The businessman got greedy and excited.

While his mind was busy with the land deal, the con man slipped in a side bet on a boxing match. The businessman handed over his cash for the bet. Then the boxer appeared to be knocked out and killed — panic! The businessman fled the scene, leaving his money behind. The "dead" boxer stood right back up. It was all fake blood.

Lesson: people don't notice the real trick when their attention is busy with something else that looks exciting or urgent.
Strategy

The gentle king who out-thought a warrior

A proud warlord refused to bow to the new king, Selassie. He agreed to meet him — but brought 500 armed soldiers, just in case it was a trap. The king welcomed them warmly, acted humble, and let the warlord feel completely in control at the meeting.

While everyone's attention was on the polite meeting, the king quietly sent another army to the warlord's camp, three miles away, and bought out all his soldiers' weapons with gold. By the time the warlord walked home, his entire army was gone — without a single shot fired.

Lesson: being extra polite and calm in one place can be the perfect cover for what you're really doing somewhere else.
In real life

The same trick, today

Here's how this shows up in an ordinary office — no kings, no con men, just three coworkers.

The real goal

Pranav really wants the Team Lead promotion. Dhanush wants the same role and watches Pranav closely for any sign of ambition.

The decoy

Pranav starts talking a lot about wanting to move into the design team instead — a totally different direction. Dhanush relaxes, thinking Pranav is no longer his competition.

The smoke screen

While Dhanush stops worrying, Pranav quietly does the real work — helping the manager Vignesh with the exact project that matters most for the Team Lead decision.

The reveal

When the promotion is announced, it's Pranav. Dhanush is shocked — he was watching the wrong door the entire time.

Dhanush thought: "Pranav's not even trying for Team Lead anymore, he's into design now. One less person to worry about." He stopped paying attention — right when it mattered most.
The toolkit

Two tools, one purpose

Tap each card to see how it works in plain terms.

+

🎯 The Decoy

Show interest in something fake so people watch that instead of your real move.

Talk openly and often about a goal that isn't your real one. People will believe it because it's so easy to check on — and while they watch that, your actual plan moves forward, unseen.
+

💨 The Smoke Screen

Act so calm, normal, and predictable that no one thinks to question you.

Instead of a flashy lie, you simply blend in — stay friendly, agreeable, unremarkable. People trust what feels familiar, so they stop looking closely, which gives you room to work.

Take away

What to do — and what not to do

✅ Do this

  • Keep your real goal to yourself until it's already done, or too far along to stop.
  • Talk openly about small, harmless plans — it keeps you looking honest.
  • Stay calm and friendly. Predictable people don't get watched closely.
  • Let your actions speak later, not your words now.

🚫 Don't do this

  • Don't announce big plans before they're ready — it gives people time to block you.
  • Don't overact or oversell your "fake sincerity" — too much passion looks suspicious.
  • Don't blurt out your feelings the moment you get excited, like the young man did with the countess.
  • Don't use these tricks to lie your way into hurting someone's trust for no real reason — this law works best as self-protection, not cruelty.
