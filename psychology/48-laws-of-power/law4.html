LAW 04 · THE 48 LAWS OF POWER

Say Less Than You Think You Should

A short guide to why quiet people seem powerful, why chatty people seem small, and how to know the difference — told simply, with real stories.

SCROLL TO BEGIN
The big idea

Talking a lot makes you look smaller. Not bigger.

It feels natural to explain yourself, fill silences, and prove how much you know. But every extra word gives people one more clue about you — your doubts, your plans, your weak spots. The less you reveal, the more mysterious and important you seem.

The moment you open your mouth for too long, you hand over your power — one sentence at a time.

Story time · Ancient Rome

The war hero who talked himself off a cliff

Coriolanus was Rome's greatest soldier. Almost nobody had actually met him — they only knew the legend. That mystery made him seem larger than life.

Then he decided to run for political office. And he opened his mouth.

Before politics

Just a legend. Scars, victories, rumors. People imagined him as a near-perfect hero because they had so little real information to go on.

Speech one

He showed up with a huge entourage, bragged about his battles, mocked his rivals, and made jokes only rich people laughed at. The crowd suddenly saw a boastful, arrogant man — not a hero.

Speech two

Trying to block free food for the poor, he lost his temper and openly insulted the idea of ordinary people having any say at all. Riots broke out.

The ending

Rome's own hero was thrown out of the city he saved. When people heard he'd never return, they threw a bigger party than after any war victory.

Every time Coriolanus opened his mouth, his popularity fell a little further. He didn't lose Rome on the battlefield. He lost it with his tongue.

Story time · Versailles Palace

The king who answered everything in three words

Nobles at the French court would argue for days about how to word a request to King Louis XIV. When they finally got their moment, he'd listen in total silence — then walk away with only a short, unreadable reply.

Nobody could ever tell what he was really thinking. So nobody could manipulate him. Try asking him something below.

Real life · Same office, three people

Meet Vignesh, Pranav, and Dhanush

Same team. Same meeting. Same big project update. Watch how differently they're seen — based only on how much they said.

V
Said too much

Vignesh

He explained every worry, every backup plan, every doubt out loud — for ten straight minutes. By the end, the room knew exactly how nervous and unsure he really was. Nobody trusted the plan anymore, not because it was bad, but because he sounded like he didn't believe in it either.

P
Said just enough

Pranav

He gave one calm sentence: "It's under control, I'll share numbers Friday." That's it. People spent the rest of the week wondering what he knew that they didn't. On Friday, he still hadn't said much — and somehow that made his results land harder.

D
Used the reversal

Dhanush

Dhanush talks constantly in meetings — jokes, small stories, friendly chatter. People think he's just a warm, harmless talker. What they miss: he's using all that noise as cover while he quietly gathers information nobody thinks to hide from him.

The exception

Silence isn't always the smart move

Going quiet on someone more powerful than you can look suspicious, not mysterious. And staying vague all the time can be read as cold or shady.

Think of the court jester — he talks and jokes constantly, so nobody suspects he's actually the smartest person in the room. Sometimes talking a lot is the disguise. The real rule isn't "never speak." It's: never speak more than you need to for the goal you actually have.

Before you go

What to actually do

Do this

  • Say the one sentence that matters, then stop.
  • Let short answers create a little healthy suspense.
  • Let others fill silences — they'll reveal themselves.
  • Think before you speak in high-stakes moments.
  • Use extra talk on purpose, as a distraction, when it helps you.

Don't do this

  • Don't explain every worry or doubt out loud.
  • Don't fill silence just because it feels awkward.
  • Don't go quiet on someone more powerful — that reads as shady.
  • Don't fire off a sarcastic reply you can't take back.
  • Don't confuse "saying nothing ever" with this rule — it's about saying only what's needed.
