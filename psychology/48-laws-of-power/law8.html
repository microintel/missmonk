Law 8 · The 48 Laws of Power

Don't chase.
Set the trap.

A short, simple guide to one of the oldest power moves there is: making the other person walk toward you — instead of you running after them.

↓ scroll to begin
In one line

Whoever forces the other person to move first is the one holding the power. If you chase, you get tired and desperate. If you wait — with something good to offer — they come to you, and you stay in control.

The story behind the idea

How an emperor was tricked into destroying himself

In 1814, Napoleon — once the most powerful man in Europe — had just lost everything. The winning nations didn't kill him. They sent him to a tiny island called Elba and let him play "king" over a handful of servants. It looked harmless. It was actually a trap.

The setup

One man, Talleyrand — Napoleon's own former minister — quietly worked behind the scenes. He didn't argue or beg anyone to lock Napoleon up tighter. Instead, he let visitors whisper to Napoleon that France still loved him and wanted him back.

The bait

Napoleon took the bait. He escaped Elba, marched into France, and reclaimed his throne — for about a hundred days. It felt like a triumph. It was actually exactly what Talleyrand wanted: to draw Napoleon out into the open, where he'd overreach, exhaust his weak country, and fall — this time for good, at Waterloo.

The lesson

Talleyrand never chased Napoleon. He never even argued his case loudly. He just planted temptation and waited. Napoleon did all the running — and all the exhausting — himself.

The big idea

The honey trap, not the hunt

A bear that knows it's being hunted is almost impossible to catch — and dangerous if it's cornered. So smart hunters don't chase bears through the forest. They leave honey in a trap and simply wait. The bear walks in on its own.

People work the same way. Chasing someone — a client, a crush, a boss's approval — makes you look eager, and eager people don't hold power. But if you build something worth wanting and simply wait, people start walking toward you.

The exception

Sometimes, waiting is the wrong move

Waiting only works if time is on your side. If you're already weaker, or the other side is getting stronger every day you delay, don't wait — that just gives them room to recover. In that case, move first, move fast, and don't give them time to think. A sudden, decisive move can catch people so off guard that they can't react properly at all.

Simple rule: if you're equal or stronger, bait and wait. If you're falling behind, strike now.

Putting it to use

What to do — and what to avoid

Do this

  • Build something genuinely valuable — a skill, a portfolio, a piece of work — before you try to attract anyone.
  • Let your results speak instead of your requests.
  • Stay calm and unbothered when someone doesn't respond right away.
  • Pick meetings and deals on your own turf whenever you can.
  • Keep your own life full, so you're never waiting on just one person or outcome.

Don't do this

  • Don't beg, over-message, or follow up so often that you seem desperate.
  • Don't argue loudly to convince people — it usually backfires and makes you look unsure.
  • Don't use fake or dishonest bait just to trap people — it damages trust fast.
  • Don't confuse "being busy chasing" with "actually making progress."
  • Don't keep waiting once it's clear the other side is only getting stronger — know when to act instead.

Stop running. Start building something worth walking toward.

The people with real power aren't the busiest ones. They're the ones others go out of their way to reach.
