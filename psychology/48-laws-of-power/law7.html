The idea

What does this law actually mean?

Don't try to do everything by yourself. Use other people's time, skill, and hard work to get things done. That part sounds fine.

Here's the uncomfortable part: once the work is finished, make sure it's your name on it. The people who actually built it? History quietly forgets them. You get remembered.

It sounds selfish. It is. That's kind of the point of this book.

A true story

The genius who did all the work — and got nothing

Nikola Tesla was brilliant. He worked eighteen-hour days rebuilding Edison's machines, was promised $50,000 for a finished job, and got a small raise instead. Edison later joked it was "American humor."

Tesla went on to build the AC electricity system the whole world still runs on. Other scientists swooped in and took credit. A man named Marconi later used Tesla's own patents to "invent" the radio. Tesla got no money. No credit.

He died poor and mostly forgotten. Edison — who once admitted, "everybody steals in commerce and industry, I've stolen a lot myself" — became one of the most famous names in history.

An old fable

The tortoise, the elephant, and the hippo

A tortoise challenges an elephant to a tug-of-war. Then he runs to a hippo and challenges him too. He gives each end of one long rope to an animal who can't see the other.

Both animals pull as hard as they can, certain they're fighting the tiny tortoise. Neither one budges. Both walk away thinking they proved how strong they are.

The tortoise never pulled the rope once. He just watched.

Two kinds of people

The Worker or the Vulture?

The book splits people in the game of power into two types. Tap to see each one.

🐢 The Worker

Does everything alone. Refuses help. Works hard, stays quiet, waits for someone to notice. Usually gets exhausted, exploited, and outrun by someone louder. This was Tesla.

🦅 The Vulture

Watches the sky. Lets others do the hunting. Steps in right when the work is finished, and calmly claims the win. Doesn't love this label — but understands the game.

The book's advice isn't "hate the vulture." It's: once you've built some strength of your own, learn to circle a little too.

This still happens today

A familiar office story

Vignesh spent three straight weeks building the project. Late nights, real effort, all his own ideas.

Pranav joined two days before the deadline. But on presentation day, he spoke the most, sounded confident, and answered every question with energy.

Vignesh sat quietly at the back. Guess whose name the manager remembered when the promotion list came out?

Watching this, Dhanush learned something. On his next project, he didn't try to build everything alone.

He formed a small team, let each person handle the part they were best at — and made sure his name was on the final report as the lead. Dhanush didn't do less work. He did smarter work, and got noticed for it.

When NOT to do this

Be careful who you take credit from

This law can backfire. If your own position isn't strong yet, grabbing credit in front of everyone just looks like theft — and people notice.

Henry Kissinger did the hard diplomatic work behind Nixon's famous visit to China. But in public, he let Nixon take almost all the credit — because Nixon was his president. Kissinger's own reputation caught up with him later anyway.

The real rule: take credit from people below or beside you. Give credit generously to people above you.

Putting it together

What to do — and what to avoid

Do this

  • Use other people's time and skills to move faster
  • Once work is finished, confidently put your name on it
  • Notice opportunities that other people's hard work is creating
  • Give credit upward, to bosses and seniors, generously

Avoid this

  • Trying to do every single thing alone — you'll burn out, like Tesla
  • Grabbing credit while your own position is still weak
  • Forgetting everyone who helped you — you'll likely need them again
  • Announcing an unfinished idea to a room full of people
