The 48 Laws of Power · Law 2

Don't trust your friends too much.
Learn to use your enemies.

Friends get comfortable and turn on you. Enemies have something to prove — and can become your best supporters. Tap and see.

Friends expect favors and rarely say the hard truth.
The Big Idea

Pick people by skill, not by love

Don't give power or big jobs to someone just because you like them. Judge by ability. People with something to prove often work harder than people who already have your heart.

Friends

  • Hide their real feelings to keep peace
  • Your favors feel like charity to them
  • Quiet envy builds up over time
  • Betrayal hits harder — you never saw it coming

Enemies

  • Expect nothing, so they're truly grateful
  • Work hard to prove themselves
  • Turn a threat into a helper — double win
  • Keep you sharp, not lazy
True Story · Byzantine Empire

The king who trusted his best friend — and lost his head

True Story

Michael & Basilius

  • Picks a friend: Young king Michael makes his best buddy his top advisor, over a smarter uncle.
  • Showers him with gifts: Money, titles, even his own wife. Trying to buy loyalty.
  • Friend turns: The friend gets him to kill his own uncle — then takes his job.
  • Betrayal: Friend refuses to repay a debt, then has Michael killed in his sleep.
He created a monster who forgot every favor — and thought he'd earned it all alone.
True Story · Ancient China

The emperor who used enemies instead of friends

True Story

Emperor Sung

  • Sees the danger: Sung knows his own "loyal" generals could betray him, just like he betrayed the last king.
  • Smart move: He gives them rich estates to retire on, far from power. No threat left.
  • Wins over a rebel: A defeated king fears Sung's wine is poisoned. Sung drinks it first. The king becomes his most loyal friend.
  • Forgives a plotter: Sung has proof another rebel was plotting against him — but frees him anyway. The rebel is stunned into total loyalty.
A person spared feels real gratitude. A friend just feels entitled.
Today's World

Same law, modern office

Vignesh

Promoted his friend, not the best person

Vignesh made his college buddy his deputy over a more skilled coworker. The friend started slacking off, expecting a free pass "because we're close" — and turned bitter when a bonus went elsewhere.

Mistake

Picked comfort over skill. Too awkward to give honest feedback later.

Better move

Keep the friendship. Give the job to whoever's actually best at it.

Pranav

Turned a harsh critic into an ally

A rival publicly trashed Pranav's product. Instead of getting defensive, Pranav bought him coffee, fixed the flaws he'd pointed out, and gave him credit.

Result

The critic became one of his best advisors — grateful, and eager to prove himself.

Lesson

An honest critic beats a friend who only tells you what you want to hear.

Dhanush

Kept work and friendship separate

Dhanush managed friends and strangers on the same team. His rule: work decisions go to whoever delivers best — friendship stays outside the office.

Result

Team trusted him — no favoritism. Friendships survived too, untested by money.

Lesson

Keep friends for friendship. Work with whoever's skilled.

Takeaway

Do this, not that

Do

  • Pick people by skill, not by love
  • Give rivals a real chance
  • Keep some distance at work
  • Notice early signs of entitlement

Don't

  • Give power just because you're close
  • Assume a friend is always honest
  • Ignore skilled strangers
  • Be shocked when a favorite turns
