LAW 5 · THE 48 LAWS OF POWER

Your name walks into the room before you do.

People rarely judge you by who you really are. They judge you by what they've already heard about you. That's your reputation — and it's the most powerful thing you own.

THE IDEA

What is a reputation, really?

Think about the last time you met someone new after hearing things about them. You didn't meet them with a blank mind. You already had a picture — "she's brilliant," "he's shady," "they're always late." That picture is a reputation, and it arrives before the person does.

”

A good name does half the work for you. A bad name makes people fight you before you've said a word. You don't fully control what people think — but you control what you give them to think about.

The old book that this idea comes from puts it simply: build a name around one clear quality — honesty, speed, kindness, brilliance, whatever fits you — and protect that name like it's treasure. Because once it cracks, everyone who was ever a little jealous, a little threatened, or a little bored will pile on.

STORY ONE · CHINA, YEAR 228

The general who won without fighting

The Empty City Trick

100 men. 150,000 enemies. Zero arrows fired.

A general named Chuko Liang was known across China as the cleverest strategist alive — people called him "the Sleeping Dragon." One day, almost his whole army was away. An enemy force of 150,000 soldiers was marching straight at his town, and he had only 100 men left to defend it.

Instead of panicking, Liang did something strange. He opened the city gates wide, told his soldiers to hide, and sat alone on top of the wall — calmly playing music, like he didn't have a care in the world.

When the enemy general arrived and saw this, he stopped his entire army at the gate. He knew Liang's reputation: a man who always had a trick up his sleeve. An open, undefended city was exactly the kind of trap Liang would set. So instead of walking in, he ordered a full retreat.

The city was empty. The trap wasn't real. But Liang's name was real enough to stop an army.
Then the enemy general just sees an old man and an open gate. He walks in, and the story ends there. Reputation was the entire trap — Liang built it over years, long before that day ever happened.
STORY TWO · NEW YORK, 1841

The showman with nothing to lose

Barnum vs. Peale

No money, no name — so he attacked someone else's name instead

A young P. T. Barnum wanted to buy a museum in Manhattan. He had a deal almost sealed, but the sellers backed out at the last second and sold it to a rival, Peale's Museum, instead — simply because Peale's had a trusted name and Barnum had none.

Barnum had no reputation to protect, so he had nothing to lose. He wrote letters to newspapers, planting doubt about Peale's finances and management. He never proved anything — he just made people wonder. The rumors spread, the stock dropped, and the panicked owners sold the whole museum to Barnum instead.

Years later, once Barnum had built his own big reputation, he used a gentler version of the same idea: he mocked Peale's "serious" hypnotism shows with a hilarious fake version of his own — making Peale's museum look outdated instead of scandalous.

Tactic: sow doubt

If nobody knows your name yet, quiet doubt is your best weapon. Once people start whispering "wait, is that true?" about a rival, they either defend themselves (which looks suspicious) or ignore it (which lets the doubt grow). Either way, attention shifts your way.

Tactic: gentle mockery

Once your name is solid, harsh attacks make you look bitter. A light, funny jab works better — it says "I'm confident enough to laugh," and it quietly makes the rival look a little stiff or old-fashioned, without you ever sounding cruel.

STORY THREE · AMERICA, 1890

The warning: an attack that backfired

Edison vs. Tesla

He tried to destroy a rival's name — and damaged his own instead

Thomas Edison believed his own electric system (DC) was the future. When Nikola Tesla's rival system (AC) started winning, Edison panicked and went to war on Tesla's reputation — publicly electrocuting animals to "prove" AC was dangerous, then pushing for the first execution by electric chair, using AC current, to scare the public.

It backfired badly. The execution was botched and turned into a horror story. People didn't just distrust AC — they started seeing Edison himself as cruel and desperate. Tesla's name recovered. Edison's own name took the bigger hit.

Going too far in an attack doesn't punish your rival. It exposes you.

PUTTING IT TO USE

What to actually do — and what to avoid

Do this

  • Pick one quality you want to be known for, and be consistent about it.
  • Let your name spread slowly, through real actions — not announcements.
  • Stay calm when rumors hit. Calm reads as confidence.
  • Once your name is solid, defend it with light humor, not heavy attacks.
  • If you're unknown and being blocked by someone else's name, doubt (used carefully) can open a door.

Don't do this

  • Don't go silent out of fear, or explode out of panic — both look desperate.
  • Don't attack harder than you need to. Overkill turns attention onto you.
  • Don't try to be known for five things at once. It waters everything down.
  • Don't assume no one's watching. Your name is always doing work, even offline.
  • Don't lean on someone else's good name forever — eventually, build your own.

There's no version of this you can skip.

You live around other people, whether you like it or not. So they will always judge you on something. The only real choice is whether that "something" is a story you built — or one someone else wrote for you.
