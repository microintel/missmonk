01 — The Geology

Why does an ice age happen?

For a century, scientists guessed: a shifting mountain range, a volcano gone quiet. Grand causes for a grand result. They were all wrong.

In the early 1900s, Serbian scientist Milutin Milanković traced ice ages to the Earth's gentle wobble toward the sun. A Russian meteorologist, Wladimir Köppen, found the real trigger hiding inside that theory: not brutal winters, but a summer that simply never got warm enough to melt last winter's snow.

The feedback loop

"It is not necessarily the amount of snow that causes ice sheets but the fact that snow, however little, lasts." — Gwen Schultz, glaciologist

Within a few hundred years, a layer of snow nobody would notice becomes a continent buried under ice. No single force did it. The same small effect, repeating on itself, did.

02 — The Investor

Buffett's secret isn't skill. It's a 75-year-old snowball.

Warren Buffett's net worth is roughly $84.5 billion. Of that, $84.2 billion arrived after his 50th birthday — and most of it after he turned 65.

Run the thought experiment Morgan Housel runs in the book: keep Buffett's 22% return, but give him a normal life — invest a modest amount in his 20s, retire at 60 to play golf. His fortune would not be $84.5 billion. It would be roughly $11.9 million — 99.9% smaller. The skill barely changed. The runway did.

Same 22% return. The only variable that changes is how long the snowball is allowed to roll.
03 — Bringing It Home

The same snowball, in rupees

You don't need Buffett's returns to see Buffett's lesson. A plain Indian SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) in an equity mutual fund, averaging a realistic 12% annually, makes the same point.

Meet two savers. Riya starts a SIP of ₹5,000/month at age 25. Aman starts the exact same ₹5,000/month SIP at age 35 — just ten years later — and both stop contributing and let it ride at 60.

05 — Why We Miss This

Linear brains in an exponential world

Add 8 nine times in your head — eight seconds, you'll get 72. Multiply 8 by itself nine times and most people freeze. The answer is 134,217,728.

Our intuition runs on addition. Compounding runs on multiplication. That mismatch is why Bill Gates, in 2004, publicly wondered why anyone would need a gigabyte of Gmail storage — and why it's why most of us underestimate a 10-year SIP and overestimate a hot stock tip. We chase a single big jump and ignore the small thing that just needs to be left alone.

Chapter 4 — In Short

Summary: The Power of Compounding

Morgan Housel's central claim in this chapter: extraordinary outcomes — five ice ages, an $84.5 billion fortune, a 100-million-fold leap in hard drive storage — rarely come from extraordinary force. They come from an ordinary effect that is allowed to feed on itself, for a very long time.

  • 01Buffett's fortune is less about being a great investor and more about being a decent investor for 75 years. Time, not skill, did most of the work.
  • 02Jim Simons earned 3× Buffett's returns but has far less wealth — because he only had half the runway. Returns matter less than how long you sustain them.
  • 03In Indian terms: a SIP started at 25 beats one started at 35 by over 3×, even with identical contributions and returns. The ten "boring" early years are the real engine.
  • 04Compounding is unintuitive because our brains think in addition (8+8+8…), not multiplication (8×8×8…). This is why we underestimate patient savings and overestimate hot tips.
  • 05The practical lesson: chase returns you can sustain for decades, not the highest return you can find for a year. Consistency that survives market crashes, job changes, and boredom beats brilliance that doesn't last.
  • 06As the book puts it — the best investing book might just be one page, titled Shut Up And Wait.
