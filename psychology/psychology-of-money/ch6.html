01
The Story

A man who was wrong almost every time

Heinz Berggruen ran from Nazi Germany with nothing, and later became one of the greatest art dealers alive. He didn't have a gift for spotting genius. He had a habit: buy a lot, hold on, and wait.

What actually happened

Berggruen bought paintings the way a fisherman casts a wide net — hundreds of them, from painters no one had heard of yet. Most turned out to be worthless. A handful were Picassos.

In the year 2000 he sold part of that collection to Germany for over €100 million. Its real value was well past a billion. One winning slice paid for every mistake, forever.

🇮🇳 Closer to home

Vignesh, an engineer in Coimbatore, buys 15–20 small-cap stocks a year through his broker app. Most go sideways or lose money. He barely remembers half their names.

One of them — a tiny logistics company nobody was watching — turned into a 22-bagger. That single stock now outweighs every loss he's ever taken, combined.

02
The Idea

A tiny sliver does almost all the work

This isn't luck or magic. It's how "long tails" work — a small number of extreme results quietly produce most of the outcome, while the crowd in the middle barely matters.

Read this curve like a portfolio: the tall hump on the left is every ordinary, forgettable bet. The thin trail stretching right, almost flat against the ground, is the one or two results so big they carry the entire outcome.

The Proof — Public Markets

The stock market isn't any gentler

You'd think buying "safe," established public companies avoids this. It doesn't. J.P. Morgan tracked the Russell 3000 — a broad index of big U.S. companies — since 1980.

Share of companies per sector that lost 70%+ of their value and never recovered, 1980–2014

40% of all companies in the index were effectively failures. Yet the whole index still grew 73-fold — because 7% of its companies produced almost all of the gain.

🎬

From Rambo to Rupees Zero

Carolco Pictures made the biggest hits of the '80s — Rambo, Terminator 2, Total Recall — and was worth $400M by 1991. Five years later, it was bankrupt. Stock: zero. This isn't a rare story. It's the normal fate of nearly 4 in 10 public companies.

You are a tail event too

It's not just which stocks you pick. It's how you behave during the scary months. A study invested $1 every month from 1900–2019 through three different mindsets.

Recessions covered barely 22% of those 119 years. Staying calm through just that sliver of time is worth nearly double, over a lifetime. Your reaction during the worst few months matters more than every calm decision you'll ever make.

07
Take It Home

How to actually live by this

Do

  • Spread bets wide — one strong winner is meant to carry many quiet losers
  • Expect most of your picks to disappoint. That's not failure, that's normal
  • Judge yourself on the process you followed, not any single outcome
  • Stay invested through the scary months — that's where the real return lives

Don't

  • Panic-sell because a handful of picks went to zero
  • Judge a strategy by its worst month or its best story
  • Expect to be right most of the time — even Peter Lynch was right 6 out of 10
  • Play it so safe you remove any chance of catching your own "Picasso"
Last Thought

The longest tail of all

Out of a hundred billion planets in our galaxy, exactly one grew intelligent life. You reading this sentence is the rarest tail event imaginable. Everything else — one bad quarter, one failed stock — is small by comparison.
