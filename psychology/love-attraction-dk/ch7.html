the pattern

It's not bad luck.
It's a pattern.

Vignesh

Every person Vignesh has ever loved has felt a little far away — busy, distracted, hard to reach.

His father was exactly the same. Always present in the house, never quite there.

In the 1980s, a therapist named Harville Hendrix noticed something strange about who we fall for. We don't pick partners randomly. Something in us reaches for people who feel like the people who raised us — good parts and difficult parts included.

No childhood is perfect. So somewhere inside, most of us are still quietly hoping: "This time, someone like that will finally choose me properly."

the old wound

Your partner isn't
the problem. The wound is.

Pranav

The moment his partner leaves for the evening, Pranav's stomach tightens. His mind starts running through worst cases.

It looks like jealousy. It isn't. It's an old fear, still wide awake.

Therapist Bruce Crapuchettes explains it simply: we're drawn to people who accidentally poke our unfinished business — because some part of us is hoping this is finally our chance to heal it.

Here's where it goes wrong: trying to fix the fear by controlling your partner. "Don't go out without me."

Here's where it goes right: saying the fear out loud instead. "I feel anxious when you leave — could you reassure me before you go?"

That one sentence is the whole difference between repeating your pain and finally closing it.

finding new safety

Notice the thread,
not the blame.

If your relationships keep hitting the same wall, gently ask yourself: what did those people have in common? Not to punish yourself — just to see it clearly.

And remember — a relationship isn't the only place healing happens. Feeling safe within yourself first makes the search for love a lot less desperate, and a lot more honest.

three keys

How to actually
talk it through

When an old wound gets triggered, most people either explode or shut down. Neither one heals anything. These three moves do.

01 — Mirroring

Repeat it back to them.

Say what you heard, in your own words. It proves you were actually listening — not just waiting for your turn to speak.

MeeraIt bothers me when you don't call — it makes me feel forgotten.
VigneshSo when I don't call, you feel like I've forgotten you?
02 — Validation

Let their feeling be real.

You don't have to agree with the opinion. But the feeling behind it is real to them — and pretending otherwise never helps.

AditiStop leaving your cups everywhere — it drives me crazy, I feel surrounded by chaos.
PranavI get it — clutter makes you feel like everything's out of control. I don't want that for you.
03 — Empathy

Step into their side, briefly.

You don't have to see it their way forever. Just for one moment — long enough to understand why it hurt.

MeeraSeeing you talk to your ex made me feel so insecure — and I couldn't even say anything without ruining the party.
VigneshThat must have been really hard — feeling that way and not being able to say a word about it.
