Coping with the lonely times — small, real ways to be kinder to yourself, one idea at a time.

Sometimes we haven't found the right person yet — and that's okay. The skill worth building isn't waiting. It's learning to treat yourself with the same care you'd want from a partner.

Here are 11 simple ideas from the science of self-compassion. Each one starts with a real-life moment, then the lesson behind it.

01 / 11

Be kind to yourself first

A date never called back. You start thinking, "I should call and tell him what I think of him."

Before reacting, stop. Ask yourself how you actually feel — instead of jumping straight to anger. This is called self-compassion: caring for yourself the way you'd care for someone you love.

02 / 11

Feel it before you react

You're angry at your partner, and "You're an idiot!" almost slips out.

Try saying "I feel angry with you right now" instead of attacking. Naming the feeling calms the reaction.

03 / 11

Notice thoughts, don't believe them

After a long dry spell of dating, you think, "I must be ugly. All the good ones are taken."

This is mindfulness: just observe the thought — "I feel insecure, I feel lonely" — without deciding it's true. Thoughts become easier to calm once you stop arguing with them.

04 / 11

Let yourself be real

Hiding your worries from a partner so you look "perfect."

Real confidence comes from an honest, realistic picture of yourself — flaws included. Denying weaknesses causes more conflict than accepting them ever could.

"Bringing people's awareness to their true self matters — it increases their sense of meaning in life."

— paraphrased from Schlegel, Hicks, Arndt & King, University of Missouri
05 / 11

Soften, allow, soothe

Your chest feels tight right after an argument.

Four small steps: relax → soften the tight spot → allow the feeling to stay → soothe yourself like you would a friend. Pain doesn't need to be fixed instantly — just held gently.

06 / 11

Build a kinder inner voice

Your inner critic snaps, "You really made a fool of yourself, didn't you?"

Swap it for a compassionate voice: "I love you and I don't want you to suffer." That critic was never your friend — you get to choose a kinder one.

"I love you and I don't want you to suffer."

— the compassionate voice, in place of the inner critic
07 / 11

Keep in touch with yourself

Writing three pages by hand every morning — chores, fears, nonsense, anything.

This is called morning pages. The point isn't good writing — it's clearing your head before the day starts.

08 / 11

Breathe through it

Nerves creep in before meeting someone new.

Sit comfortably, and follow the feeling of air moving in and out. Thoughts will drift — that's fine. Just keep coming back to the breath.

09 / 11

Send kindness outward

Wishing a close friend well, then a stranger on the street, then even someone you find hard to like.

This is loving-kindness meditation: "May I be well, may I be safe, may I be happy" — said first for yourself, then for others. It builds real ease in the world.

"May I be well. May I be safe. May I be happy."

— loving-kindness meditation
10 / 11

Practice kindness in the mirror

Before you shower or get made up — at your most natural self — looking into your own eyes.

Say your own name, ten times: "[Your name], I love you exactly the way you are." Your brain needs to hear that voice too.

11 / 11

Let yourself laugh

Watching something funny after a hard day.

Laughter really does raise your pain threshold. A good laugh isn't just a distraction — it gives your whole nervous system a boost.
