a short guide to falling (or not falling) in love

Why do some people fall in love fast — and some never quite feel it at all?

It's rarely about meeting "the right person." Most of the time, it's about the story we already carry inside us — long before that person ever showed up.

↓ keep scrolling ↓

It's not them. It's the lens you're looking through.

Some people fall in love within a week of meeting someone. Others date person after person — each one lovely, each one wrong — and never quite feel that spark settle into something real.

The difference usually isn't luck, and it isn't the partner. It's the attachment style we bring into the room — the quiet set of expectations we've built from every relationship that came before this one.

Let's meet two people who show this perfectly.

Anxious attachment

Pranav falls fast — maybe too fast

Pranav matched with someone on a Friday. By Sunday, he was already picturing weekends together. He replies within seconds, worries when texts slow down, and reads every short reply as a sign that things are falling apart.

This isn't weakness — it's a pattern. When we feel a little unsure of our own worth, we tend to grab onto love quickly, before we've really checked if it's a good fit. Excitement gets mistaken for connection.

"If someone likes me, I don't want to lose them" — Pranav, three dates in, already planning the future.

It's not always wrong — sometimes the person really is right. But moving fast without checking for real trust and closeness first is how heartbreak sneaks in later.

Avoidant attachment

Vignesh is still "waiting for the one"

Vignesh has dated plenty of great people. Somehow, none of them were ever quite right. He still thinks about his ex sometimes — funny how he only remembers the good parts now.

Deep down, staying single feels safer than getting close. Holding out for a flawless "perfect person" — or staying attached to someone who's already gone — means Vignesh never has to risk being hurt by someone real, flaws and all.

"She was great, but something just felt missing" — Vignesh, about the fourth person this year.

He genuinely wants love. But real love means loving an imperfect person up close — and that's exactly what feels unsafe.

Secure attachment

The quiet third option

Not everyone swings between rushing in and running away. Some people can sit with uncertainty, give a relationship time to grow, and still walk away calmly if it isn't right — without spiralling or clinging.

This is what real, lasting love is usually built on: patience, trust, and the ability to see a partner clearly — not too idealised, not too feared.

So what actually makes "love," love?

Psychologist Robert Sternberg says every kind of love is really just a mix of three ingredients. Depending on how much of each you have, you get a completely different kind of relationship.

Intimacycloseness & warmth alone → simple liking
Passionon its own → hot, quick infatuation
Commitmenton its own → cold, distant empty love
All three together= complete, lasting love

Mix the ingredients differently, get a different love

Sternberg's triangle isn't just theory — it plays out in real relationships all the time. Here's what each combination actually looks and feels like.

🤝 Intimacy only

Liking

Closeness and warmth without the spark or the promise. This is your closest friendship — the kind you'd trust with anything, minus the romance.

⚡ Passion only

Infatuation

"Love at first sight" — all heat, no history. Thrilling in the moment, but there's no closeness or promise underneath it yet to hold it up.

🕯️ Commitment only

Empty love

Staying together out of habit or duty — with the closeness and the spark both long gone. Two people sharing a life, not really sharing each other.

💞 Intimacy + Passion

Romantic love

Closeness and chemistry together — the whirlwind, butterflies-and-late-night-talks stage. What's still missing is the promise to stick around.

🏡 Intimacy + Commitment

Companionate love

A deep friendship built to last a lifetime. Often what long marriages settle into once the early spark softens into something steadier.

💍 Passion + Commitment

Fatuous love

A whirlwind romance and a quick commitment — think a courthouse wedding after a three-week fling — without ever really getting to know each other.

✨ Intimacy + Passion + Commitment

Consummate love

All three at once: closeness, attraction, and commitment working together. Sternberg calls this "complete" love — the kind most people are quietly hoping to find, and the kind that takes the most work to keep.

So what's love that actually lasts really built on?

Not excitement alone. The research keeps pointing to the same three quiet habits — none of them flashy, all of them essential.

01

Patience

Real closeness takes time to build. Letting it grow — instead of rushing to lock it down or fleeing the moment it gets uncertain — is what gives it room to become real.

02

Trust

Trust is what lets two people stay open with each other instead of guarding themselves. Without it, even the most passionate connection stays fragile.

03

Seeing each other clearly

Not as flawless, and not as a threat — just as a real person, with real strengths and real flaws. Love that survives is love that was never based on an illusion in the first place.

The real takeaway

Whether you fall in love in a week like Pranav, or you're still "waiting for the one" like Vignesh — neither means you're broken, and neither means the other person is wrong for you.

It just means it's worth pausing to ask: am I feeling this because of them — or because of an old fear I'm still carrying? That one honest question is usually where real love finally has room to grow.

— here's to loving a little more clearly, and a little less anxiously 💛
