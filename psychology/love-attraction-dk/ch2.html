Where it started

One quiet experiment changed everything

After World War II, a psychiatrist named John Bowlby noticed something simple: kids who felt safe with their parents grew into adults who felt safe in love. He named it attachment theory.

Years later, his student Mary Ainsworth tested it. She placed a toddler and mom in a playroom, then had mom step out for a few minutes. What the child did next — cry, ignore it, or calmly cope — told her everything about how that child expected the world to treat them.

Secure kidsPlayed happily, missed mom, calmed down fast when she returned.
Anxious kidsBarely explored, cried hard, took ages to settle again.
Avoidant kidsLooked totally fine outside — but their heart was racing.

Putting two together

When two hearts meet

Secure + Anxious

The secure partner's steadiness lets the anxious one finally relax.

Secure + Avoidant

Given space without judgment, the avoidant partner slowly stops running.

Anxious + Avoidant

One chases closeness, the other retreats from it — and the chase only makes the retreat worse. It's the most common, and most exhausting, pairing of all.

A gentle note 💛

Some people carry both anxious and avoidant traits at once, often from a genuinely difficult past. If that's you, be kind to yourself — it's a hard combination to carry alone, and a good therapist can make a real difference.
