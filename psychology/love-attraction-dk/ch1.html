01 — An old story, new feelings

Love and marriage haven't always been a couple.

For most of history, romance and marriage were two separate ideas. The earliest marriage contract we've found is 2,500 years old — and it describes a 14-year-old girl being exchanged for six cows. Not exactly a love story.

The Ancient Greeks agreed. They had four different words for love, and none of them meant "romance."

Agápelove for the soul
Érosphysical desire
Philíawarm friendship
Storgēfamily affection

The idea of falling head-over-heels in love only shows up later, in medieval tales of knights and courtly romance — stories that eventually grew into the modern novel. It took even longer for psychologists to connect the dots: the way we loved as babies shapes the way we crave love as adults.

02 — Where it all begins

Long before romance, we needed connection just to survive.

Some baby animals can walk within minutes of being born. Human babies can't even hold up their own heads. We arrive completely helpless — which means our very first survival skill isn't running or hiding. It's getting someone to care for us.

The smile that keeps us alive

A newborn's smile isn't just cute — it's a survival tool. It hooks a tired, exhausted parent and keeps them coming back. Psychologist John Bowlby, who first studied this bond in 1957, argued that babies who smile the most are the ones who get the most love and care in return.

What happens without it

In one striking study from the 1940s, more than a third of children raised in an orphanage died before turning two — not from hunger or illness, but simply from a lack of holding, playing, and attention. Connection, it turns out, is as essential as food.

03 — The switch to grown-up love

We never outgrow the need to attach — we just redirect it.

Here's the simple truth: we don't lose our need for connection when we grow up — we just move it. The bond we once had with a parent gets transferred to a new person: a romantic partner.

Falling in love and becoming a parent are the two biggest brain-reshaping events of a human life.

Just like a caring parent helps a baby feel secure, a healthy loving relationship can make an adult calmer, more confident — and, surprisingly, more independent, not less.

04 — Why our brains grew for this

Big babies, big brains, big love.

In the wild, most mammals aren't loyal to one mate — they spread their genes as widely as possible. Even chimps, our closest relatives, tend to leave all the childcare to the females.

Humans went a different way. Over 2.5 million years, our brains tripled in size to handle complex social life, tools, language — and the hard work of keeping love alive. But bigger brains meant bigger heads, and bigger heads meant babies had to be born early, before they were ready for the world.

The result? Human infants need enormous amounts of care — so much that survival favored parents who stuck together. Some scientists even think this is why humans started walking on two legs: freeing up arms to carry a helpless baby. We didn't just evolve to love. We evolved because we loved.
