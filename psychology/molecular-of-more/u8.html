The Opening Story

Meet Andrew — the man who had everything, except what he wanted

Case Study · Andrew

Andrew was a top-performing enterprise software salesman in his twenties — confident, outgoing, successful. Outside of work, he spent his time pursuing one-night stands, having slept with over a hundred women. Yet he longed for real intimacy. He knew it mattered for his long-term happiness. He knew his pattern wasn't getting him there.

He kept doing it anyway.

💡 The key insight: Andrew's rational mind knew what he needed. But something older and more powerful kept overriding it — his dopamine desire circuit, wired not for long-term happiness, but for immediate wanting.

Andrew isn't unusual. This is the same system operating in all of us. To understand why we do what we do — and why knowing better isn't enough — you need to understand dopamine.

Dopamine's Function
Drives motivation and wanting — not pleasure. It's the signal that says "Go get that." Not "That feels good."
Common Misconception

Dopamine is not the "pleasure chemical"
— it's the wanting chemical

Most people think dopamine = pleasure. The book makes a crucial distinction. Dopamine fires before you get the thing — not during. It creates desire and motivation, not satisfaction. Here's what separates them:

✦ Dopamine IS
🎯 The drive to pursue and acquire
⚡ Activated by potential, not possession
👁️ Triggered by seeing something attractive
🔮 Focused on the future, not the present
📢 The "Wake up. Pay attention." signal
🍩 Why you want the donut even when full
✗ Dopamine is NOT
😊 The feeling of satisfaction after getting it
🍽️ Hunger — that's a present-moment signal
🧠 A conscious choice you make
✅ The reward itself
❤️ Love or attachment (that's different circuits)
🛑 Controllable by logic alone

Key quote from the book: "The sensation of wanting is not a choice you make. It is a reaction to the things you encounter."

Why It Evolved

The circuit evolved to keep you alive —
even at the cost of your goals

The dopamine desire circuit wasn't designed for modern life. It was designed for the savanna, where food was scarce and every opportunity could be your last. Here's the mismatch:

Evolutionary Past

🦴 Survival on the brink

Our ancestors lived most of their lives near starvation. Eating when food was available — even if not hungry — was life-or-death smart. Dopamine said "eat now, survive later."

Modern Day

🍩 Abundance everywhere

The same circuit fires for donuts, social media likes, online shopping, and endless content. Food is everywhere, but your brain still says "acquire more." The logic made sense then. Now it makes you fat, addicted, and distracted.

Dopamine is always focused on acquiring more with an eye toward future survival. It constantly scans the environment for new sources of food, shelter, mating opportunities, and resources. This was brilliant for 200,000 years. For modern life, it needs to be understood and managed — not blindly followed.

The Book's Best Example

The woman, the savanna,
and the berry bush

The book tells a vivid story that perfectly illustrates how the dopamine circuit works in real time. Follow along:

Story Walkthrough

Our ancestor's morning walk

01 A woman walks the savanna. Clear morning. Birds singing. She's walking without truly seeing — mind wandering, on autopilot.
02 She stumbles upon a bush covered in berries. She's passed it dozens of times before — it never had berries.
03 Dopamine fires. Her eyes sharpen. Her wandering thoughts vanish. Concentration snaps into focus. She scans back and forth across the bush, taking in every detail.
04 Excitement wells up inside her. Not because she's hungry. Because the future just became more secure — this bush makes food. The circuit screams "This is important. Remember this."
05 A dopamine-tagged memory is formed. From now on, every time she sees this bush, dopamine gives a little hit — a hint of alertness, a nudge of excitement. The better to motivate her to return.
💡 The lesson: Dopamine doesn't just drive behavior in the moment — it encodes importance. Things linked to dopamine release get remembered, revisited, and obsessively pursued. That's why habits form. That's why cravings return. That's why Andrew couldn't stop.
Apply to Life

What to do with this knowledge

Understanding your dopamine circuit doesn't make you immune to it. But it gives you a crucial tool: the ability to recognize it in action and make a conscious choice rather than being swept along.

⚡ Recognize the trigger

When you feel a sudden urge — to eat, scroll, buy, check — ask: "Is this hunger or is this dopamine?" Naming it creates a pause between signal and action.

🧲 Design your environment

Dopamine fires at the sight of triggers. Remove the triggers. Don't rely on willpower — it's fighting a million years of evolution. Change what your eyes can see.

🔁 Hijack it for good habits

Dopamine encodes importance through memory. Link your desired behaviors to novelty and reward. Make the berry bush the habit you want to return to.

💔 Understand Andrew's trap

Knowing what you need ≠ being able to do it. Dopamine is not logical. Changing deep patterns requires changing the circuit — not just the thinking. Get help, change the environment, build new dopamine-linked memories.
