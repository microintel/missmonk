01  —  Neuroscience

One Brain,
Two Worlds

Neuroscientist John Douglas Pettigrew discovered that the brain divides all of reality into two territories — and uses different chemicals to navigate each one.

Zone 1
Peripersonal Space

Within arm's reach. What you can touch, taste, hold, and squeeze right now. The world of the actual — pleasure, pain, reality.

Here-and-now chemicals → Serotonin, Oxytocin, Endorphins
vs
Zone 2
Extrapersonal Space

Everything beyond your reach. Three feet or three million miles away. The realm of possibility — desire, ambition, imagination.

Future-oriented molecule → Dopamine

Distance is linked to time. Anything beyond your reach can only be obtained in the future — which means it requires effort, planning, and anticipation. That gap between wanting and having is precisely where dopamine lives.

02  —  The Chemical

Dopamine

"Dopamine has a very specific job: maximizing resources that will be available to us in the future — the pursuit of better things."
— The Molecule of More

Dopamine doesn't reward. It anticipates. It fires hardest before you get what you want — fading the moment you receive it. It is the engine of wanting, not of having.

Evolution wired us this way: food you don't yet have matters far more than food already in your hands. Chasing it could save your life. Sitting still with it cannot.

The signal grows strong in pursuit. It dissolves on arrival.

03  —  The Illusion

Glamour
Is a Lie

Glamour is a beautiful illusion that promises to transcend ordinary life and make the ideal real. It depends on a special combination of mystery and grace. Too much information breaks the spell.

— Virginia Postrel

Glamour is dopamine misfiring. It makes you believe the extrapersonal is more beautiful than it can ever be once it becomes peripersonal. The farther away, the more perfect. The closer you get, the more ordinary.

✈️

Only things out of reach can be glamorous. Only things that remain unreal.

04  —  A Story

Samantha
& Demarco

The theory made human. Samantha hadn't felt it in months — that electric dopamine surge. Then, unexpectedly, an old flame appeared.

Samantha ran into Demarco — her last serious boyfriend before Shawn. They hadn't seen each other in years. She found him as funny and smart as ever, and in minutes she was starry-eyed again. Here was something she hadn't felt in a long time: the surge of excitement and the sense of possibility with a man who seemed full of fresh things to discover.

The first thing he shared was how excited he was to be engaged. His fiancée was "the one."

After he left, Samantha decided this was a good day to drink. She loved Shawn, she really did — or did she? They had been in a rut for most of a year. That feeling with Demarco was what she wanted. She had once had it with Shawn, but not anymore.

The surge wasn't about Demarco. It was about distance. He was extrapersonal — unavailable, mysterious, full of possibility. Shawn was peripersonal. Real. Present. And dopamine had moved on.

Finding love and making love stay require vastly different skills. The dopamine thrill of romance — of wanting what you don't have — will always fade. The question is what you choose to build in its place.

Many people make the transition. They outsmart the seduction of dopamine. But it requires understanding what's happening — and choosing presence over pursuit.
