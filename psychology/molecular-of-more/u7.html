The Infinite Why

Every action has a reason.
But where does it end?

Children do it instinctively. Philosophers do it on purpose. The "Why?" game exposes that most of what we do is not the real goal — it's a step toward something else. Follow the chain long enough and you reach the root.

Action I'm going to work today.
↓
Why? Because I need to make money.
↓
Why? To pay my bills and keep the electricity on.
↓
Why? Because I want comfort, security, stability.
↓
Why? Because those things make me feel good.
↓
✦
Happiness
Aristotle's answer — the final destination of every chain of "why."

This isn't just a philosophical exercise. Try it on your own habits, purchases, relationships. The chain always terminates at a feeling — not a fact, not an object, not a status.

The Philosopher's Conclusion

Aristotle's answer:
Everything is for Happiness

Aristotle — Nicomachean Ethics
"Is there not some end of the things we do, which we desire for its own sake? Will not knowing it have a great influence on life?"

Aristotle observed that while we do most things for the sake of something else, there must be a thing we seek for itself alone — something that doesn't need justification. He called it Eudaimonia (happiness, flourishing).

It's convincing. We pay bills → feel secure → feel happy. We brush teeth → avoid dentist → feel okay → happy. We suffer for worthy causes → feel proud → still, in some strange way, happy.

Aristotle was onto something universal. But here's the twist — if happiness is the goal, why don't we always choose the happiest option?

The Problem

Except we don't choose
rationally. At all.

🎲
"I just fell into it."
Most people don't choose careers through systematic comparison. They fall in — through chance encounters, gut feelings, whatever opportunities showed up first. Rational maximization is exhausting and rarely done.
🏫
College on a gut feeling
Ask people why they picked their college. The honest answer is often: "It just felt right." Not a spreadsheet. Not a happiness optimization. A vibe.
🧠
Rational decisions are costly
Weighing all options takes enormous mental effort. And it rarely delivers the relief of certainty. So instead, we just do what we want — and justify it after.

The brain doesn't run a happiness calculation before every decision. It runs on want — a messier, faster, more irrational system.

The Core Tension

The Donut Dilemma:
wanting two opposite things at once

Here's the sharpest example in the chapter. You see a donut. Two things happen simultaneously:

🍩
WANT to eat it
Dopamine. Pleasure. Craving. Reward circuitry firing.
VS
🚫
WANT to NOT eat it
Goals. Identity. Self-image. Long-term thinking.

Both are real wants. Both are happening in the same brain, at the same moment. This isn't weakness or stupidity — it's the fundamental design of the human mind. We contain contradictions.

And this isn't just about food. It's about:

📱
Want to use phone / want to be present
You know you should put it down. You also know you want to scroll. Both are real.
💤
Want to sleep in / want to hit the gym
7 AM you and 10 PM you have very different priorities — same person, different wants.
🗣️
Want to speak up / want to avoid conflict
You want honesty and you want peace. The brain doesn't resolve these — it just picks one in the moment.
Apply to Life

What to do with
this knowledge

Understanding that your brain runs on competing wants — not rational happiness maximization — is genuinely useful. Here's how to use it:

🔍 Run the Why Chain

Before a big decision, ask "why?" repeatedly. Uncover what you actually want — not the surface action.

⚡ Name the Conflict

When you feel stuck, identify the two wants fighting each other. Naming them reduces their power over you.

🌅 Design for Tired-You

Your 7 PM self is different from your 7 AM self. Set up environments so the right choice is the easy choice.

🎯 Ask: What do I actually want?

Not what you should want. Not what others want for you. What do you want? Then trace the Why to verify it's real.
