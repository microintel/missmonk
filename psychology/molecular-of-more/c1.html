The Discovery

Dopamine is not about feeling good.

Scientists used to call it the "pleasure chemical." But they got it wrong. Dopamine is really about wanting — the excitement you feel before you get something, not after.

1957
Year Found
A scientist named Kathleen Montagu found dopamine in the brain for the first time, in a hospital near London.
0.0005%
Of Brain Cells
Almost no brain cells make dopamine — but those few cells control a huge part of how we act and feel.
≠ Joy
The Big Surprise
When scientists tested food instead of drugs, dopamine fired while waiting for food — not while eating it.
∞ Want
What It Really Does
Dopamine pushes you to go after things — food, love, goals, new ideas. It is about the chase, not the reward.

Dopamine fires when you see a gap between what you have and what you could have. Once you get the thing you wanted, dopamine stops. It only works when something is still possible.

You First Notice Someone

Shawn sees Samantha across the bar. He does not know if she likes him. That uncertainty is exactly what makes dopamine fire. Every word they share feels exciting because anything could happen.

They talk easily — gym, fitness apps, shared interests. The brain reads all of this as: "something good might be coming."

Dopamine ↑↑↑ Adrenaline ↑

You Can't Stop Thinking About Them

Shawn had not dated anyone in 7 months and 3 days — and he knew the exact count. That is his brain fixating on what it does not have. Once he has Samantha, other women stop catching his eye completely.

Every time he thinks of her, he gets excited about seeing her again. He is not really in love yet — his brain is just hooked on the possibility of more.

Dopamine ↑↑↑↑ Serotonin ↓

You Make it Official

He proposes. She says yes. Now the outcome is certain. And right there — that is when dopamine starts to quiet down.

The brain got what it was chasing. So it stops chasing. Other brain chemicals like oxytocin take over now — these are the ones that create warmth, safety, and real closeness.

Dopamine → Oxytocin ↑

The Spark Gets Quieter

A few months after the honeymoon, things feel normal. Samantha smiles at a stranger. Shawn notices other women again. Neither wants to cheat — their brain just needs something new to get excited about.

"This happened in my last relationship too," thinks Samantha. Yes — because it happens to everyone. It is not a sign something is wrong. It is just how the brain works.

Key Ideas

4 things to remember.

🎯

Wanting is not the same as liking

Dopamine makes you chase things. But once you get them, the excitement can disappear. You wanted it badly — yet it feels flat now.

🔄

Sure things are boring to the brain

When you know you will get something, dopamine stops firing. The thrill is in not knowing. Once you know — it is over.

🧪

It is not your fault

When the early excitement in a relationship fades, that is not a problem with you or your partner. It is just how the brain is built.

🌱

Real love uses different chemicals

Long-term love runs on oxytocin and serotonin — the calm, happy, safe feeling. Dopamine is only for the early chase.

What To Do

How to use this in real life.

Dopamine is not bad. It pushes you to grow, try new things, and connect with people. The problem is when you keep chasing the excitement of early love — instead of building something real.

Now that you understand why the wave rises and falls, you can make smarter choices about love, habits, and goals.

✓ Good moves

  • Try new things with your partner — new places, new activities keep things fresh
  • Use the "chase" feeling to set goals that are just out of reach
  • When the early excitement fades, do not panic — it does not mean love is gone
  • Build closeness through touch, time together, and small daily care

! Watch out for

  • Leaving relationships just because they no longer feel "new"
  • Confusing early excitement with real love
  • Social media, junk food, and gambling all use the same dopamine trick
  • Giving up on good things just because the excitement wore off
