Section 01

Dopamine is your early-warning radar — not a pleasure chemical

Most people think dopamine = pleasure. That's wrong. Dopamine is an anticipation and wanting signal. It fires before you get something, not while you're enjoying it. Its job is to make you pursue anything that might help you survive.

Dopamine System
Wanting

Fires in anticipation. Lives in the future. Creates hunger, energy, excitement, pursuit. Shuts off once you get what you wanted.

H&N System
Liking

Serotonin, Oxytocin, Anandamide. Lives in the present. Produces satisfaction, warmth, peace — but only if you're actually paying attention.

Dopamine is like the little old lady who always buys toilet paper — it doesn't matter if she has a thousand rolls. You can never have too much.

This is why the man on a diet still wants a hamburger even when he isn't hungry. His dopamine doesn't care about need. It cares about potential resource acquisition. Always has. Always will.

Section 02

The Chase: When Wanting Collapses on Arrival

Case Study · Andrew

Andrew pursued attractive women obsessively. The moment a woman agreed to come home with him — the chase over — he lost all interest. She even looked different to him in an instant. By the time they arrived at his apartment, he didn't want to be there anymore.

This isn't a character flaw. It's dopamine behaving exactly as designed. The anticipation circuit was running full-blast during the chase. The moment pursuit ended, dopamine switched off. The H&N system was supposed to take over — but Andrew never developed the ability to enjoy the present, so the crash felt like a void.

Phase 1 — Dopamine Peak
Sees attractive woman. Dopamine fires. Everything else fades to gray. Total focus. Energy, enthusiasm, imagination.
Phase 2 — Hunt Mode
The pursuit itself maintains dopamine. The future possibility keeps the signal alive. He tells himself he'll resist — but dopamine wins.
Phase 3 — Collapse
She says yes. Chase ends. Dopamine shuts down instantly. Future becomes present. H&N system has nothing to catch the fall.
Phase 4 — What Could Have Been
If Andrew had trained his H&N attention — presence, noticing, real connection — the liking could have met the wanting. But it wasn't there.

The book notes something even more extreme: "He was losing interest the moment she agreed." Not even on arrival — just the confirmation was enough. The future collapsed into present-tense reality, and dopamine vanished.

Section 03

Wanting and Liking are produced
by two different brain systems

This is the core insight of the chapter. You assume that the things you desperately want will be things you enjoy having. That assumption is almost always wrong. The wanting circuit is just a salesman — it makes promises the liking circuit has to fulfill. And the salesman doesn't care if the product delivers.

Anticipation → Reality Transition
Slide from pure wanting to pure liking and see what happens in the brain
◀ Wanting (Dopamine) Liking (H&N) ▶
You haven't gotten what you want yet. Dopamine is surging — imagination is vivid, the future looks ideal, energy is high. But you're not tasting anything real.
The Office — Deangelo Vickers, on cake
Deangelo I, for one, love the corners. [eats a handful] Why did I just do that? It's not even that good. I don't even want it.
Deangelo [throws it away, then reaches back in] No. You know what? I've been good. I deserve this.
Deangelo What am I doing? C'mon, Deangelo! [yells at the cake] No! No!

This is not a joke — it's a textbook dopamine loop. Wanting fires before touching the cake. Liking is absent once he has it. Yet wanting fires again because the cake remains as a future possibility. He is arguing with his own dopamine.

The same thing drives doomscrolling (next post might be great), online shopping carts you never buy from (browsing feels better than owning), obsessive planning (trip research more exciting than the trip), and relationship fantasies (imagined intimacy more thrilling than real presence).

Section 04

Buyer's Remorse is a Neurochemical Crash

You've always been told buyer's remorse is about fear of wrong choices, guilt, or being sold to. The real explanation is more precise: the desire circuit broke its promise.

Dopamine told you that buying that expensive car would overcome you with joy and your life would never be the same. Except it didn't happen. It was never in a position to deliver that.

Once you buy the car, it moves from extrapersonal space (far, future, dopaminergic) into peripersonal space (near, present, H&N territory). The dopamine thrill evaporates. Whether you feel good now depends on whether your H&N system finds genuine satisfaction in the actual object. Usually: not enough to compensate.

Notice how a new computer or jacket can avoid this trap — they stay partly dopaminergic because they point to a future: "I'll be more productive... I'll look great when I wear this." They maintain anticipatory value even after purchase.

Section 05

Three ways out of the dopamine trap

These are the book's three explicit strategies. Only one of them actually solves the problem.

1
Chase the high

Keep buying more. Keep pursuing. Dopamine stays elevated through perpetual novelty. Effective short-term. Escalating cost long-term.

Recommended
3
Train H&N transition

Strengthen the ability to shift from dopamine desire into real liking. Presence. Mindfulness. Savoring. The hardest path — but the only lasting one.

2
Buy less

Avoid the crash by avoiding the acquisition. Minimalism and restraint. Reduces the cycle but doesn't teach you how to enjoy what you already have.

The book is unambiguous: there is no guarantee that what you desperately want will be something you enjoy having. The wanting and liking systems don't consult each other. You have to consciously bridge them yourself.

Section 06

How to actually use this in your life

🔍
Notice the gap between wanting and liking in real time

When you're excited about something, ask: "Am I actually enjoying this, or am I just anticipating it?" Dopamine-driven excitement feels real but is future-oriented. Catching this gap is the first move.

📱
Recognize the doomscroll loop

You're not enjoying the scroll — you're searching for the next dopamine hit. Each swipe is Andrew walking to his apartment. The wanting is real; the liking never comes. Put the phone down before the next swipe, not after.

🛒
Use the cart cooling rule

Leave items in your shopping cart for 48 hours. You're testing whether the H&N system will sustain the desire after dopamine fades. Most cart abandonment is healthy — your real liking system correctly rejected the dopamine salesman's pitch.

🍽️
Train your H&N attention daily

Eat one meal with full attention — no phone, no planning tomorrow. Notice flavor, texture, temperature. This is H&N training. You're building the circuit that makes the present actually satisfying, not just theoretically nice.

💬
In relationships: catch the "she looked different" moment

When a relationship moves from chase to commitment, or from new to familiar — and suddenly seems less exciting — that's the dopamine-to-H&N handoff. Most people mistake this neurochemical transition for falling out of love. It's not. It's the moment the real relationship can begin.

🎯
To travel hopefully is better than to arrive — use this against yourself

When you notice yourself planning the next thing while experiencing the current thing (chewing food while thinking about tomorrow), you've been hijacked. Name it. Return. The present is the only place H&N can operate.
