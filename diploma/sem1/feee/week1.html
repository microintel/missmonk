Meaning & General Precautions

Electrical safety refers to the practices and measures implemented to prevent injuries and damage caused by electricity — protecting people from shocks, burns, and fire through proper training, correct equipment, and strict adherence to safety standards that mitigate risks from high voltage, arcing, and electromagnetic fields.

▶ Watch: General electrical safety precautions

Workplace precautions

  • Lockout / Tag-out: de-energize machinery during maintenance or repair.
  • Regular inspection: routinely check tools, cords and devices for wear or damage.
  • Proper training: all personnel trained in protocols and task-specific hazards.
  • PPE: insulated gloves, safety glasses, flame-resistant clothing at all times.
  • Signage: visible warning signs and labels near high-voltage areas.
  • Dry conditions: keep work areas free of moisture.
  • Safe practices: never operate equipment in wet conditions or with damaged cords.

Using measuring instruments

  • Use DC meters for DC circuits and AC meters for AC circuits.
  • Connect DC meters with correct polarity and proper range.
  • Always select meters of the correct range.
  • Connect Ammeters in series; Voltmeters in parallel with supply/load.
  • Lay meters flat on the working table to avoid parallax error.
  • Never meddle with connections while power is ON.

Electrical Personal Protective Equipment

Electrical PPE is protective equipment used by workers exposed to hazards at voltages above 50 V AC/DC. Employees must be provided appropriate PPE and trained in its selection, use, limitations, inspection, and maintenance.

  • Hand protection: rubber gloves prevent injury from live parts or arc-flash burn.
  • Foot protection: leather footwear prevents injury from contact with live parts.
  • Hearing protection: inserts protect ears from sound pressure above 140 dB during an arc blast.
  • Eye protection: face shields and goggles guard against flash injury.
  • Head & face protection: non-conductive hard hats prevent injury from shock or arc-flash explosion.

Disposal of waste

  • Collect all waste materials.
  • Identify and segregate: cotton waste, metal pieces, chemical waste, electrical waste.
  • Segregate into saleable and non-saleable waste.
  • Send non-saleable material (cotton, paper, wood) for disposal by burning.
  • Segregate saleable items — Aluminium, Copper, Iron, Screws, nuts — and send to stores.
  • Clean the floor if oil has been spilt.

Electrocution & Rescue Technique

Electrocution is a severe or fatal injury from electric shock, occurring when significant current passes through the body. Severity depends on voltage, current intensity, exposure duration, and current path through the body.

▶ Watch: Freeing a person from electrocution

Steps to free a person

  1. Do not touch the victim directly while they are still in contact with the source — you risk being shocked too.
  2. If power cannot be turned off immediately, use a non-conductive object (wooden stick, plastic tool) to separate the victim from the source.
  3. Turn off the source of electricity if possible; otherwise use a non-conducting pole of wood, cardboard, paper, or plastic.
  4. Once removed from the source, check for breathing and pulse; administer CPR if trained, until emergency personnel arrive.
  5. Call emergency medical services and the electricity supply company helpline immediately.
  6. Never approach a victim electrocuted by a high-voltage line — electricity can arc several feet through the air.

CPR sequence (if trained)

  • 1. Check the airway
  • 2. Clear the airway
  • 3. Check for breathing
  • 4. If not breathing, begin compressions
  • 5. 30 chest compressions
  • 6. 2 rescue breaths — alternate until help arrives
05

Moving Injured People During Emergency

A) By a single person

Protecting the spine: do not move someone with a suspected spinal injury. Stabilize the head with towels or pillows on both sides to prevent rolling. Provide first aid, like CPR, without moving the head. Only roll the person onto their side if they are in immediate danger (vomiting or choking on blood) — and only with at least one other person to prevent the body twisting.

  • Human crutch method: if the person is conscious and can move, and has an injury to only one leg.
  • Drag method: safer than lifting — drag by leg, arm, or clothing depending on injury type.
  • Cradle method: for carrying a child.
  • Backpack carry: for carrying a larger person.

B) By two people

Check the person for neck and back injuries first. Leave the person on the ground to start. Put your bodies in the proper position, pull the person to a sitting position, then assist them to their feet. Wrap your hands around the injured person's waist, or place their arms over your shoulders, and move out together.

  • Handed seat / swing carry — for a back or leg injury victim
  • Fore and aft method — for an unconscious victim
  • Arm carry (alternate) — for an unconscious victim

C) Alternative carries

  • Improvise a stretcher from tree branches or straight pole-like objects with a large cloth, blanket, or bedspread.
  • Fashion a stretcher using four interlocked hands.
  • Carry the person with a chair, or construct a "chair" using your hands.

Electric Shock — Effects & First Aid

Electric shock is the physiological/biological reaction or injury caused by electric current passing through the human body.

Effects of current on the body

1 mA

Just a faint tingle.

5 mA

Slight shock felt — disturbing but not painful.

6–25 mA (women)
9–30 mA (men)

Painful shock; muscular control is lost.

50–150 mA

Extremely painful shock. Death is possible.

1–4.3 A

Heart pumping action becomes arrhythmic. Death is likely.

10 A

Cardiac arrest and severe burns occur. Death is probable.

First aid steps

  1. Turn off the power before touching the victim, to prevent further injury.
  2. If the power can't be turned off, use a dry wooden stick, rubber gloves, or non-metallic object to separate the person from the source.
  3. Call emergency services (emergency no. 112) immediately if unconscious or severely burned.
  4. Check breathing and pulse; begin CPR if the victim is unconscious and not breathing.
  5. Treat burns: cool with running water for 10–15 minutes. Do not apply ice, ointments, or creams — cover with a sterile non-stick dressing.
  6. Prevent shock: lay the person down, elevate their legs, and keep them warm if pale, weak-pulsed, or breathing rapidly.
  7. Look for internal injuries even without visible external burns — seek medical attention immediately.
  8. Do not apply grease or oil to burns; cover flash burns with a dry sterile dressing and bandage — do not cool them. Keep the victim from getting chilled.
  9. Do not let a crowd gather around the person — let them get fresh air.

Electrical Fire, Prevention & Extinguishers

An electrical fire starts from a malfunctioning or faulty electrical component — wiring, an appliance, or an outlet — where overheating from excessive current ignites nearby flammable material.

Causes

  • Short circuits from damaged or aging wiring.
  • Overloaded circuits drawing too much power.
  • Faulty wiring installations causing sparks.
  • Old or damaged appliances with worn-out cords.
  • Loose connections in outlets/switchboards generating heat.
  • Improper use or overloading of extension cords.

Precautionary measures

  • Regular electrical inspections of wiring, appliances, and outlets.
  • Avoid overloading circuits — use multiple circuits within safe limits.
  • Use surge protectors against voltage spikes.
  • Replace frayed or exposed wiring immediately.
  • Install circuit breakers and fuses.
  • Unplug unused appliances.
  • Educate employees and household members on safe practices.

Rescue technique during a fire hazard

  1. Pull the nearest fire alarm as you exit the building.
  2. Ensure there is no fire danger on the other side before evacuating.
  3. If there's smoke, stay low, keep a hand on the wall, and crawl to the nearest exit.
  4. Once clear, call your report contact and inform them of the fire.
  5. Go to your refuge area and await instructions from emergency personnel.
  6. Notify the proper authorities once you are safe.

PASS Method

  1. Pull the safety pin.
  2. Aim at the base of the fire.
  3. Squeeze the handle to release the agent.
  4. Sweep the nozzle side to side until the fire is out.

Electrician Tools & Functions

Common hand tools and instruments used in electrical maintenance and installation work.

1. Cutting Plier

Cuts wire and holds/grabs it; has an insulated handle.

2. Long Nose Pliers

Holds wires in small spaces; cuts and tightens small nuts.

Strips insulation from stranded or solid copper wire.

7. Ball Peen Hammer

Used for striking punches.

8. Line Tester

Tests and identifies the phase / live wire / conductor.

9. Clamp-On / Tong Tester

Measures high current by magnetic induction.

10. Drilling Machine

Makes round holes or drives fasteners.

11. Angle Grinder

Cuts metal conduit pipes and electrical panels.

12. Heat Gun

Shrinks heat-shrink tubing and removes paint from surfaces.

A flexible ruler used to measure size or distance.

Combines voltage, current, and resistance measurement functions.

Copper or aluminium insulated wire with twisted strands — PVC, VIR types common.

16. Insulation Tape

Safety tape used to cover and insulate cables and conductive materials.

Earthing (Grounding)

▶ Watch: Earthing — definition, necessity, types & applications

Earthing (grounding) connects the metal body of an appliance or machinery to the earth's conductive surface through a low-resistance wire, to prevent electric shock and ensure safety.

Whenever a live wire contacts the body of an appliance, it is connected directly to earth, bringing the body voltage to zero — so the operator gets no shock on contact. During lightning, high voltage induced in overhead lines and tall buildings is safely discharged to earth through the grounding wire, protecting buildings, transformers, and machinery.

  • Prevents electric shock by diverting excess current safely into the ground.
  • Protects appliances from voltage fluctuations and damage.
  • Prevents fire hazards from short circuits.
  • Ensures stable, balanced voltage levels in the system.
  • Improves overall safety of electrical installations.

Types of earthing

TypeDescription
Plate EarthingCopper/GI plate buried >3 m deep, embedded in alternate layers of charcoal, sand & salt. Costlier — used for precise equipment like computers, control rooms.
Pipe EarthingGI pipe buried >3 m deep. Cheap and the most common form — used for domestic, commercial, industrial, and transmission/distribution earthing.
Rod EarthingA copper or GI rod driven deep into the earth. Common in substations, distribution networks, and individual installations.
Strip / Wire EarthingConductive copper or GI strips buried for industrial use — residential buildings, plants, telecom, power plants.
Chemical EarthingSpecial compounds enhance conductivity and reduce resistance — used in hospitals, data centers, telecom towers, substations.
Grid / Mat EarthingA mesh of rods connected by copper conductors, reducing overall grounding resistance and limiting potential gradient.

Procedure — Plate earthing

  1. Excavate an earth pit of 900 mm × 900 mm × minimum 3 m deep (or 1 m × 1 m × 3 m). Level the bottom.
  2. Use a GI plate of minimum 600 mm × 600 mm × 6.3 mm (or copper plate, min. 3.15 mm thick).
  3. Fix two 50 mm × 6 mm GI strips to the plate with nut, check-nut, bolt and washer at two points each; weld and join at the top.
  4. Fix a 19 mm GI pipe with a wire-mesh funnel at the top for watering, to keep the area around the plate moist.
  5. Fill around the plate with alternate layers of charcoal and salt; backfill the pit with stone-free soil.
  6. Build a 450×450×450 mm inspection brick chamber over a PCC layer, 100 mm above ground, covered with a cast-iron lid.

Procedure — Pipe earthing

  1. Make a borehole 500 mm or 1 m diameter, 3.5–3.75 m deep as per approved drawing.
  2. Lower a 38/45/50 mm diameter GI perforated pipe electrode (3 m or 2.5 m long) attached to a 19 mm GI pipe with a wire-mesh funnel; a GI strip or 12.7 mm pipe acts as the earthing connection, with 8 SWG GI wire passed through it.
  3. Fill the space between electrode and borehole wall with alternating layers of coke/charcoal and common salt up to 2.5 m.
  4. Build a 450×450×450 mm brick inspection chamber over a PCC layer, 100 mm above ground, with a cast-iron cover.
