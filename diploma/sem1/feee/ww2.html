I

Electric Current

The continuous flow of free electrons through a conductor in one direction. I = Q / t

rememberFLOW
More electrons per second past a point = more current.
Unit: Ampere (A)
Meter: Ammeter · live current → Tong/Clamp tester
Symbol: I
Formula: I = Q / t
V

Voltage / Potential

Electric pressure at a point, measured with reference to earth or the source's negative terminal.

rememberPRESSURE
Taller water column → higher pressure → water sprays farther, just like higher voltage pushes charge harder.
Unit: Volt (V)
Meter: Voltmeter
Symbol: V
Analogy: water pressure pushing current the way height pushes water
R

Resistance

Opposition offered by a material to the flow of electrons through it.

rememberOPPOSITION
A narrower neck (higher R) squeezes the path — fewer electrons get through per second.
Unit: Ohm (Ω)
Meter: Ohmmeter / Multimeter (low–med) · Megger (high R)
Symbol: R
Analogy: a pinch in a pipe slowing water down
PD

Potential Difference

The difference in electrical pressure between any two points — current flows from higher to lower potential.

rememberTWO-POINT GAP
Tank A sits higher than Tank B — that height gap is the potential difference.
Unit: Volt (V)
Meter: Voltmeter
Rule: current flows point of higher potential → point of lower potential
Note: once levels equalise, PD = 0 and flow stops
EMF

Electromotive Force

The force that keeps circulating current in a circuit — the source's continuous push, not a one-time drain.

rememberTHE PUMP
The pump lifts charge from − back to + internally, against the field — that continuous push is EMF. Stop the pump and the flow dies, unlike a one-off PD drain.
Unit: Volt (V)
Meter: Voltmeter
Sources: Battery, RPS, DC generator, AC generator
EMF vs PD: EMF is the source's push; PD is what you measure between two points because of it
